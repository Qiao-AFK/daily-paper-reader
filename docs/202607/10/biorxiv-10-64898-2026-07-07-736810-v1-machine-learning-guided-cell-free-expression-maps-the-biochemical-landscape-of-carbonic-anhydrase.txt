Abstract 1Carbonic anhydrases are among the fastest known biocatalysts, reversibly facilitating the hydration 2of CO 2 to HCO 3- at rates up to 10 7 s-1 , which warrants their investigation for industrial carbon 3

capture technologies. However, engineering carbonic anhydrases to maintain stability under 4

harsh industrial process conditions remains a key challenge, and sequence-to-function datasets 5

compatible with machine learning to inform forward engineering are lacking. Here, we developed 6

a high-throughput platform that couples cell-free gene expression with a gaseous CO 2 colorimetric 7assay to map the fitness landscapes of carbonic anhydrases. From 96 diverse natural homologs, we 8identified a robust variant from the Aquificota phylum and conducted an exhaustive mutational 9scan and functional assessment of this enzyme at 70°C and 90°C, covering >99% of all single-10 amino acid substitutions (totaling 4,365 mutations assayed in 39,285 reactions). This biochemical 11 landscape was used to benchmark 22 zero-shot protein fitness models and identify critical 12 mutations that improved enzyme stability at 90°C by more than three-fold. We then used both 13 zero-shot protein language models and supervised learning to filter 419 model-generated variants 14 from a ProteinMPNN library of 100,000 sequences, leading to a best-in-class enzyme that retained 15 activity after incubation at 95°C. This work demonstrates that integrating cell-free enzyme 16 engineering with machine learning enables opportunities for high-throughput experimental 17 measurements to benchmark and improve protein language models, accelerate design loops, and 18 expand functional exploration within protein families where experimental information is limited. 19 20

Introduction 1

Carbonic anhydrases catalyze the hydration of CO 2 into bicarbonate (HCO 3-) at diffusion-limited 2rates 1–3 . This hydration reaction is a central component of natural mineral carbonation through 3rock weathering, a process in which bicarbonate reacts with various metal ion sources to precipitate 4permanent metal carbonates 4, thereby removing ~1 gigaton of CO 2 per year from the 5atmosphere 5,6 . Given rising CO 2 emissions that contribute to anthropogenic climate change 7,6carbonic anhydrases have been explored as a potential catalyst for large-scale CO₂ removal in both 7direct air capture (DAC) and flue gas sequestration. However, the industrial deployment of these 8enzymes has been hampered by the requirement for high-performance catalysis under elevated 9temperatures (>70°C) and alkaline pH typical of industrial carbon capture cycles 1–3 .10 11 Numerous protein engineering campaigns have sought to improve the stability of carbonic 12 anhydrase variants to bridge this gap. These strategies include bioinformatics searches for carbonic 13 anhydrases from known thermostable species 8–10 and rational site-directed mutagenesis 11–14 .14 Despite progress, past efforts have been biased towards a relatively small subset of the 15 phylogenetic landscape. Furthermore, previous screens have often relied on proxy assays, such as 16 hydration in CO 2 saturated water 15 , esterase activity 16 , or supplying bicarbonate and measuring the 17 reverse reaction 14 , which fail to accurately reflect the nuances of capturing gaseous CO 2 as a 18 substrate under industrial conditions. Thus, engineering a robust carbonic anhydrase capable of 19 meeting the demands of industrial CO 2 removal remains an outstanding challenge. 20 21 In recent years, enzyme engineering has been reshaped by machine learning (ML) approaches 17 ,22 

de novo design tools such as ProteinMPNN 18 and RFDiffusion 19 , and protein language models 23 (pLMs) 20 . In conjunction with off-the-shelf tools that can be used for function-agnostic tasks, 24 building supervised models with labeled protein sequence-to-function data from high-throughput 25 functional screens can improve optimizing a specific objective. However, a key challenge is 26 measuring data of multiple dimensions (e.g., activity and thermostability) at a scale and speed that 27 captures both successes and failures to inform forward engineering. To date, the lack of open-28 source, large-scale sequence-to-function mappings for carbonic anhydrases has limited the ability 29 of deep learning architectures to expand beyond sequence space sampled by nature. 30

4Here, we developed a high-throughput platform that combines cell-free gene expression (CFE) 1and a pH-based colorimetric assay to rapidly map various sequence-to-function landscapes of 2carbonic anhydrases ( Fig. 1A-C ). We used this platform to explore a broad range of natural 3carbonic anhydrases at elevated temperatures, identifying a highly thermostable cluster within the 4

Aquificota phylum. We then performed an exhaustive deep mutational scan, testing 99.9% (4,365 5of 4,370) of the single-amino acid mutants of the carbonic anhydrase from Aquificota bacterium ,6mapping the functional activity of >99% single amino acid substitutions across three different 7temperatures. We utilized this comprehensive dataset to provide mechanistic insights into the 8enzyme, identify key residues that confer significant improvements in stability, and benchmark 9various zero-shot fitness predictors. In parallel, we used deep learning sequence redesign via 10 ProteinMPNN to combinatorially vary larger portions of the enzyme, surveying candidates further 11 from the wild type in sequence space while minimizing inactive variants. By integrating iterative 12 rounds of ML-guided selection and generative modeling, we identified redesigned variants that 13 surpass best-in-class natural and engineered enzymes. Overall, our results indicate that changes to 14 protein function are readily accessible through the combination of cell-free prototyping and deep 15 learning sequence redesign. 16 17 18 19 20 21

Exploring the natural sequence space of carbonic anhydrases to identify an optimal engineering 2

4We began by surveying the literature for natural carbonic anhydrase sequence diversity to identify 5a robust starting point for engineering. Specifically, we selected 5 candidates spanning three 6distinct classes (alpha, beta, and gamma) reported to demonstrate activity at temperatures above 760°C. These enzymes were expressed in crude extract-based CFE reactions, and their activity was 8evaluated in both cell-free lysates and as purified enzymes. For functional screening, we employed 9a modified version of the Wilbur Anderson Assay 15,21 that measures gaseous CO 2 hydration in 10 high-throughput ( Fig. S2 ). The carbonic anhydrase from Persephonella marina significantly 11 outperformed all other reported thermostable homologs tested ( Fig. S3 ). 12 13 To systematically explore the natural sequence diversity surrounding the P. marina carbonic 14 anhydrase, we identified the 1,000 most similar UniProt sequences by BLAST (BLOSUM62, gap 15 penalty score = 11, e-value = 10 -5 ) and constructed a sequence similarity network (SSN) using the 16 Enzyme Similarity Tool from the Enzyme Function Initiative 22 (67% similarity cutoff; Fig. S4 ). 17 From this SSN, we selected 96 carbonic anhydrases covering all major subnetworks, including 18 representative sequences belonging to the P. marina subnetwork, for cloning as linear templates 19 and expression via cell-free synthesis. Following expression, we measured CO 2 hydration directly 20 in cell-free lysate across 7 CO 2 concentrations and 4 pre-incubation conditions ( Fig 1D, Figs. S1, 21 

S4 ). Of these 96 enzymes, 41 retained measurable activity at ≤15% v/v CO₂, and 7 of these 22 remained active when measured after a 24 h incubation at ~70°C. Embedding all sequences using 23 Evolutionary Scale Modeling-2 (ESM-2) showed that the thermostable variants cluster tightly in 24 latent space, whereas only four cluster together in the SSN ( Figs. S4-5 ). The best-performing 25 natural enzyme, isolated from an Aquificota species, exhibited an in vitro >2.8-fold improvement 26 in activity over the P. marina carbonic anhydrase after 6 h at 70°C ( Fig. S6 ). Benchmarking protein language models against an experimentally complete fitness landscape 9

pLMs have recently been shown to excel at enzyme functional annotation 24,25 ; yet, their ability to 10 predict user-specified, quantitative performance metrics remains poorly understood due to a 11 scarcity of comprehensive experimental datasets. It is additionally difficult to know a priori which, 12 if any, modeling methods might correlate with the objective function of interest (e.g., enzymatic 13 activity with a target substrate, catalytic rate, thermal stability, etc.). To this end, we leveraged our 14 carbonic anhydrase deep mutational scan to assess the predictive accuracy of multiple zero-shot 15 scoring methods. Across the complete mutational landscape and 20 zero-shot fitness predictors, 16 we found modest but distinct correlations (Spearman's rank correlation coefficient, ρ, up to 0.49; 17 

Fig. 2F ). Notably, SaProt recovered the top 2 confirmed stabilizing mutations (N178M and C51R) 18 within the top 22 predictions of the 4,371-variant library ( Fig. 2G ). Only two additional models 19 (ESM-1v, n = 152; and ESM2, n = 350) were able to recover the top 2 stabilizing mutations among 20 the top 10% of their rank order ( Fig. 2G, Fig. S12) . ProSST, which has performed well in the 21 broader ProteinGym zero-shot benchmark 26 demonstrated poor predictability (ρ = 0.235, Top 2 22 recovery n = 2493) . Structure-aware predictors (i.e., SaProt, ESM-IF, ESM3) tended to perform 23 best across both metrics, suggesting that stability determinants are largely structural and exhibit 24 limited epistasis in this enzyme. However, ProteinMPNN, which is also structure-aware, 25 performed poorly, indicating that architectural differences among models strongly influence zero-26 shot behavior. 27 28 Notably, the Spearman rank correlation coefficient poorly predicted a model’s ability to recover 29 the top stabilizing mutations ( Fig. S13 ). We therefore evaluated multiple complementary metrics, 30 including the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC), the normalized 31 discounted cumulative gain (NDCG), and position-averaged scores ( Fig. S14–19 , Table S1 ). 32 Predictive power varied across distinct structural domains: mutational effects on stability were 33

MPNN-guided design of a non-thermostable carbonic anhydrase 6Considering that our complete site-saturation mutagenesis (SSM) dataset of AQ-WT demonstrated 7that the enzyme possesses high mutational tolerance, we hypothesized that carbonic anhydrases 8are excellent targets for combinatorial redesign. To survey this higher-dimensional mutational 9space, deep learning inverse-folding models such as ProteinMPNN have been shown to improve 10 both the stability and activity of various proteins by introducing broad combinatorial sequence 11 variations (>30% mutational frequency) 18,27,28 . Thus, we next leveraged these inverse-folding 12 architectures to design highly stable carbonic anhydrase variants. 13 14 As a testbed for ProteinMPNN design, we first selected a non-thermostable Gammaproteobacteria 15 carbonic anhydrase (Gb-CA). We chose Gb-CA because of its proximity to AQ-WT in our 16 sequence similarity search ( Fig. 1C ) despite its poor stability at 70°C ( Fig. 3A ), providing a larger 17 dynamic range for optimization. To begin, we generated 60 computationally designed variants 18 across a range of ProteinMPNN hyperparameters, varying the sampling temperatures (T = 0.1– 19 1.0) and fixed-residue counts (30–70 amino acids, corresponding to 13-31% of the most highly 20 conserved sites across the gene sequence, defined using EVCouplings conservation 29 ). Five 21 sequences were generated per combination of sampling temperature and fixed residues ( Fig. 3B ). 22 All 60 sequences were predicted to fold well using AlphaFold 2 (pLDDT (Predicted Local 23 Distance Difference Test) >90, pAE (Predicted Aligned Error) <5; Fig. S20 ). Experimental 24 evaluation showed that 31 designs were active without incubation and 11 remained active after 24 25 h at 70°C ( Fig. 3C ). Higher sampling temperatures (T = 0.5–1.0) led to widespread inactivation, 26 consistent with excessive diversification. 27 28

Supervised Machine Learning Methodologies 25 

All supervised training, hyperopt, and evaluation was conducted using AIDE 64 . For the first round 26 of supervised training protein sequences were encoded using the ESM2-3B model 27 (esm2_t36_3B_UR50D). Embeddings were extracted from the final layer and processed using three 29 pooling strategies across variable positions: flattening (concatenating all position embeddings), 30
