Abstract 17 Plastids house the biology of eukaryotic photosynthesis. The majority of a plastid’s proteome is imported 18 after cytosolic translation, but a few dozen proteins on average remain organelle-encoded, translated by the 19 plastid’s own ribosomes. While 1000s of plastid genomes have been sequenced, the availability of less than 20 ten proteomes and only two species with full 70S plastid ribosomal structures limit our understanding of 21 land plant evolution. To address this, we optimized a protocol for the rapid isolation of 22 

Marchantia polymorpha chloroplasts that provides a highly enriched and intact organelle fraction from 23 gradient volumes as little as 2 mL. Our approach was successfully applied to six other species, including 24 

Chlamydomonas reinhardtii and Nicotiana tabacum . Focusing on M. polymorpha , we determined the 25 proteome of the chloroplast fraction, identifying 1337 nuclear-encoded proteins with a high confidence, 26 where 83% belong to orthologs shared with angiosperms. We further isolated large protein complexes by 27 RNA affinity purification using poly-lysine and provide the high-resolution structures of the 50S subunit of 28 the chloroplast ribosome and RuBisCO from this bryophyte using cryogenic EM and image reconstruction 29 to 2.23 and 2.12 Å resolution, respectively, highlighting the structural conservation of both complexes. For 30 chloroplasts, our data show that the genome reduction event experienced by the common ancestor of 31 bryophytes has had little impact on the organelle’s complexity and that they underscore a high level of 32 structural conservation of core components of plastid biology. Our data provide novel resources and 33 methods to explore the functional evolution of plastid proteomes and major macromolecular complexes of 34 cyanobacterial origin. 35 36

Introduction 38 The transition from a cyanobacterial endosymbiont to the different types of plastids of algae and plants 39 entails the loss and transfer of most of the endosymbiont’s genetic information 1–5 . Crop plants such as pea 40 or maize hence encode more than a thousand proteins in their nuclear genome that need to be imported by 41 their plastids after cytosolic translation 6,7 . Plastid proteomes are complemented by a few proteins that are 42 kept organelle-encoded, maybe due to the need for the co-localisation of genes of the electron transport 43 chain and the bioenergetic membrane itself for functional redox regulation 8–10 , and protein hydrophobicity 44 impairing membrane translocation 11–14 . Subunits required during the early steps of 70S ribosome assembly 45 are likely retained due to constraints associated with securing proper ribosome maturation 15 . Typically, as 46 is the case for the 70S ribosome, plastid-encoded proteins interact with nuclear-encoded ones to form 47 macromolecular complexes such as photosystem I and II, the NADH dehydrogenase complex, the ribulose-48 1,5-bisphosphate-carboxylase/-oxygenase (RuBisCO) enzyme, and ATP synthase. 49 A significant event in plastid evolution was the transition from water to land 16,17 . Plant terrestrialization and 50 the origin of the Embryophyta is characterised by the emergence of the cuticle, stomata, spores and seeds, 51 multiplastidy, the adaptation of stress response networks already present in the streptopyhte algal ancestor, 52 a step-wise expansion of genomes, and a general increase in mitochondrial and plastid proteome size 17–23 .53 An early-branching and ecologically important group of land plants are the bryophytes, uniting liverworts, 54 hornworts, and mosses (Fig. 1). After genome expansions at the origin of the Chloroplastida and again at 55 the origin of Streptophyta 24,25 , the bryophyte ancestor experienced genome reduction that drove the 56 lineage’s phenotypic evolution 19 . Now, after more than 500 million years, bryophytes are characterized by 57 highly derived genomes that in sum encode a larger variety of gene families than their vascular relatives 26 .58 The positioning of bryophytes on the phylogenetic tree provides an ideal opportunity to explore the 59 trajectory of plastid biology after genome reduction in comparison to vascular plant plastids. 60 Experimentally curated plastid proteomes and high-resolution structures of macromolecular complexes 61 outside of a few angiosperm model systems, however, are scarce (Fig. 1). To address this, we optimized a 62 chloroplast isolation protocol that requires minimal resources and time. It yields intact chloroplasts from a 63 diverse set of species and eases their study, enabling chloroplast biology exploration across a broad variety 64 of taxa. Focusing on the liverwort Marchantia polymorpha, we obtained a highly enriched chloroplast 65 proteome, which corroborates previously predicted evolutionary trajectories of land plant plastid 66 proteomes 20 . With the aim of isolating chlororibosomes, we subjected the plastid fraction to an RNA affinity 67 purification using poly-lysine 27 . This resulted in the enrichment of multiple macromolecular complexes 68 from the isolated chloroplasts. Using cryogenic electron microscopy (cryo-EM), we resolved the structures 69 of the 50S ribosomal subunit and RuBisCO at respective resolutions of 2.23 and 2.12 Å. Our data provide 70 new resources to study plastid biology and allow us to hypothesize that the chloroplast proteome and 71 ribosome of the bryophyte were not significantly affected by genome reformatting at the origin of the 72 bryophytes. 73 74

Lineage-specific gene family expansions contribute to the M. polymorpha plastid proteome 135 Comparative proteomics is an important component in understanding organelle evolution and the 136 contribution of the compartment to the ecophysiology of a plant. Ancestral state reconstruction of 137 mitochondrial and plastid proteomes across a billion years of chloroplastidal evolution recently underscored 138 the expansion of organelle proteomes from algae to angiosperms 20 . It predicted an intermediate chloroplast 139 proteome size for M. polymorpha and we find that 85.6% of the experimentally-curated nuclear encoded 140 chloroplast proteins were predicted based on orthology with previously experimentally characterized 141 proteins (Fig. 3b). Apart from corroborating the identity of individual chloroplast proteins, it also shows 142 that an orthology-based approach to predict an organelle proteome has merit for plotting broader 143 evolutionary patterns in the absence of an experimentally-curated proteome. Hence, we clustered the 144 genomes of 57 diverse chloroplastidal species (Table S3) into orthogroups following the approach used 145 previously 20 and focused on 4,797 plastid orthogroups (POGs). The structures of the 50S ribosomal subunit and RuBisCO demonstrate a high level of 183 

macromolecular complex conservation 184 

With the aim of determining a chlororibosome structure for M. polymorpha , we subjected the purified 185 organelles to RNA affinity purification by poly-lysine (RAPPL) 27 . The eluate of this purification was used 186 for mass spectrometry, which showed an enrichment of all chlororibosome subunits, the ribulose-1,5-187 bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase (RuBisCO) enzyme, and proteins of PSII, PSI, and the ATP synthase 188 (Table S4). The eluate was used for cryo-EM grid preparation and subsequent single-particle reconstruction. 189 2D classification revealed several large protein complexes including recognizable views of the 190 chlororibosomal large 50S subunit (cLSU), RuBisCO, the soluble F1-ATPase complex, and a chaperone 191 (Fig. S4). Of these, there were sufficient varied views of RuBisCO and the 50S for three-dimensional 192 reconstruction. No particle classes containing the full 70S or the small 30S ribosomal subunit were 193 identified. 194 We reconstructed high-resolution density maps of the cLSU and RuBisCO to respective resolutions of 2.23 195 and 2.12 Å, reported at the 0.143 Fourier shell correlation cut-off 51 (Fig. 4a & 5a, S4-5). These maps were 196 used for atomic modeling using previously published sequences 52–58 . The ribosomal protein (RP) structures 197 were first predicted using AlphaFold3 41 and then fitted and refined into the experimental map. The rRNA 198 was mutated from a Spinacia oleracea model (PDB ID 5MLC 59 ). The quality of the density map allowed 199 for modeling 6 rRNA modifications in the cLSU 23S rRNA chain (Fig. S6). Out of the 6 modifications, 5 200 were identified previously in Chlamydomonas and Escherichia coli structures, whereas the remaining 201 modification, U579 to 5-methyluridine, appears to be novel to Marchantia (Fig. S6) 60–62 . Alignment of the 202 spinach 50S to the Marchantia 50S revealed only 5 subunits out of 31 with a root mean square deviation 203 (RMSD) above 3.0 Å. These were L5, L13, L21, L33, and PSRP6, with RMSDs of 3.7, 8.1, 3.1, 3.2, and 204 4.5 Å respectively. The values reported for L5 and L33 are due to variation in the position of a single small 205 loop region in each. The main difference between PSRP6’s across the structures is that 9 more C-terminal 206 residues were modeled in the Marchantia model, however these residues are also present in the spinach 207 PSRP6 sequence, so this represents a difference in flexibility or map quality. The main noticeable difference 208 between the Marchantia and spinach models was found at the surface near the exit tunnel, where RPs L13, 209 L21, and L22 are present (Fig. 4b). In S. oleracea , these RPs have regions at their N- and C-termini that 210 stick out from the surface and they were hypothesized to form an interface with the thylakoid membrane 59 .211 Comparisons of structure and sequence suggest that the C-terminal region was extended (e.g. L21) or gained 212 (e.g. in L22) by the ancestor of eudicots, since they are absent or shorter in other chloroplastidal species, 213 including M. polymorpha (Fig. 4b). The N-terminal sequence of L13 is present in the annotation of the 214 Marchantia protein but does not appear in the density map (Fig. 4b). Overall, while the amino acid 215 sequences for chloroplastic RPs between the S. oleracea and M. polymorpha 50S share on average 68% 216 identity, they exhibit highly similar structures through conserved folds (Table S5). 217

Here, we have developed a rapid and efficient plastid purification protocol that is effective across six tested 256 plant lineages. By combining our plastid isolation approach with a RAPPL protocol to enrich chloroplast 257 protein complexes, we provide high-resolution structures of the large subunit of the plastid ribosome and 258 RuBisCO from M. polymorpha . Together with the plastid proteome, our high-resolution structures of two 259 key protein complexes present a unique window into land plant evolution, as bryophytes are characterized 260 by an ancient genome reduction event and a subsequent gene family expansion independent from that of 261 vascular plants 19,26,72 .262 The oldest fossil record of a plastid is that of the filamentous red alga Rafatazmia chitrakootensis . Their 263 rhodoplasts were captured in 1.6-billion-year-old rock formations of the Tirohan Dolomites from central 264 India 38 . At that point in time, these red algae (rhodophyte) plastids had already evolutionarily diverged from 265 those of their common ancestor of Archaeplastida, from which the Glaucophyta and Chloroplastida (the 266 green lineage) also evolved 73 (Fig. 1). Ever since, the photosynthetic organelles of algae and plants have 267 experienced constant adaptations, with the transition to a terrestrial habitat unequivocally representing a 268 groundbreaking step for the evolution of life. 269 Bryophytes occupy an evaluative position in the evolution of land plants. They are the sister-clade to all 270 vascular land plants 72,74 , have retained ancestral features such as flagellated sperms or a dominant, 271 photosynthetic haploid gametophyte 75–77 , and their genomes have independently diverged from those of 272 vascular plants after having been reduced in their common ancestor 19,26 . We find the Marchantia chloroplast 273 proteome to comprise just over 1300 proteins, of which more than 80% are represented by orthologs in 274 angiosperms. The large number of orthologs speaks for a trade-off between ancient nuclear genome 275 reduction and the need to retain – or even expand – gene families needed for the complexity of plastid 276 biology that had to adapt to a terrestrial habitat. We predict that additional organelle proteomes from 277 bryophytes will support this pattern. 278 Annotating the M. polymorpha plastid proteome finds almost 200 proteins of unknown function, with only 279 sporadic domains being identifiable in some cases. TPR and PPR proteins were particularly enriched. TPR 280 domains are key for protein-protein interactions and are involved in chloroplast chlorophyl biogenesis, 281 photosystem assembly and intracellular chloroplast distribution 78–81 . PPR domains are crucial for plastid 282 RNA editing and organelle-targeted members of this protein family expanded with the origin and 283 diversification of land plants, probably to compensate for an increase in mutations caused by higher levels 284 of UV radiation 20,82–85 . Theory has it that complex thalloid liverworts such as M. polymorpha lack mRNA-285 editing possibly due to secondary loss 86,87 , but recent results on Cyathodium cavernum revealed a minimum 286 of 129 canonical C-to-U mRNA editing sites, suggesting that this is not a universal trait of the taxon 88 .287 Studies thus far describe on average 100 proteins with PPR (or related RNA processing) domains encoded 288 by liverwort genomes 89,90 , though their intracellular localization was not clear. We find 35 proteins 289 (including 22 with unclear functions) with such domains among the chloroplast proteome of M. polymorpha 290 – a species that evidently does not edit its plastid-encoded genes in the same way that C. cavernum does. 291 This serves as a reminder that studying diversity often challenges generalizations and it raises the question 292 why dozens of PPR domain-containing proteins are then imported. This constrains modern evolutionary studies that, in addition to primary 300 sequences work with protein structures 100,101 , are used for the design and optimization of semi-synthetic 301 enzymes 102–104 . Our study deposits structures for 30 bryophyte proteins and 3 rRNAs that constitute two 302 key super complexes of cyanobacterial origin, the large subunit of the chlororibosome and the hetero-303 oligomeric form I of RuBisCO with its eight small and large subunits. The RAPPL proteome does contain 304 proteins of the small subunit of the chlororibosome (Table S4) and, in combination with the absence of 305 additional assembly factors on the cLSU structure, it suggests the 70S chlororibosome disintegrated during 306 preparation. 307 Several ribosomal proteins such as L2 to L6, L14, and L16 are among the very few reliable phylogenetic 308 markers across the diversity of life 105 . The main reasons are two-fold: their conserved core structure over 309 4 billion years of evolution and an almost strict vertical inheritance even in prokaryotes 63,106–108 . The 310 bryophyte’s chlororibosome shares this overall level of structural conservation, but there are noticeable 311 differences concerning the absence of terminal loops in L13, L22 and L23 when compared to the spinach 312 chlororibosome (Fig. 4). The extended loops in L22 and L23 appear eudicot-specific, however, and are 313 hypothesized to form an interface with the thylakoid membrane 59 . Their functional relevance in eudicots 314 remains to be demonstrated, along with proteins that might functionally replace these regions in the 315 liverwort. The absence of the L13 N-terminal extension in the density map – while being present in the 316 annotated gene – implies either a high degree of flexibility, disorder or potentially a post-translational 317 cleavage event. Whatever the reason, this region of the bryophyte’s chlororibosome seems to better 318 resemble its ancestral roots than that of its eudicot relatives on land. 319 RuBisCO takes a special place in evolution. It is likely the most abundant protein structure on Earth and 320 the vast majority of biomass owes its origin to it, yet it remains one of the least efficient enzymes we know 321 109–111 . It also appears resistant to change. Its macromolecular structure has not been noticeably modified 322 across the vast diversity of photosynthetic eukaryotes since its origin as a hetero-oligomeric complex in 323 cyanobacteria some 3 billion years ago 106 (Fig. 5). The evolution of RuBisCO witnessed the rise of O 2, a 324 molecule that lowers its efficiency by several orders of magnitude. Despite a strong selection pressure for 325 its active center to exclude oxygen, RuBisCO remained unaffected and, instead, distinct carbon capture 326 mechanisms evolved 110 . When introduced into E. coli , not a single nucleotide mutation was observed after 327 150 chemostat generations for the two genes encoding RuBisCO, despite the prokaryote’s biochemistry 328 mutationally adapting to use CO 2 as its sole source for synthesizing hexose, pentose and triose sugars 329 instead 112 . Thus, RuBisCO presents us with an extreme case of an enzyme that is part of a functional 330 module, in which proteins with many interaction partners or those at the center of critical metabolic fluxes, 331 change in sequence significantly slower than their partners 113,114 . The enzyme’s resilience to environmental 332 changes is telling and might explain why its biotechnological optimization remains kind of a Sisyphean 333 task. 334 The combination of our plastid isolation and the RAPPL protocol, together with cryogenic EM offers a 335 powerful toolset for exploring the structural proteome of large, soluble protein complexes of the plastid 336 stroma. The application of this workflow in a single species illuminates how plastid proteomes have evolved 337

Plant growth conditions 347 

We used ecotype Takaragaire-1 (Tak-1) of the liverwort Marchantia polymorpha throughout the study. The 348 wild type gemmae were grown under continuous light (70 μmol m -2 s-1 ) on half-strength Gamborg B5 349 vitamin agar-plates (GVA). 350 

Plastid isolation 351 

The plant tissues were lysed using mortar and pestle, in the presence of 5-10 mL isolation buffer (IB; 50 352 mM HEPES KOH pH 7.5, 0.33 M sorbitol, 1 mM MgCl 2, 1 mM MnCl 2, 2 mM EDTA pH 8, 0.5% w/v 353 BSA, 0.5% w/v PVP40, and freshly added 5 mM ascorbate). The lysate was filtered through a pre-soaked 354 (in IB) Mira cloth and centrifuged at 1000 g, 10 min. The pellet was resuspended in 200 μL IB and loaded 355 onto a gradient of 70% Percoll ® (400 μL at the bottom of the tube) and 30% Percoll ® (1 mL overlayed). 356 Density gradient centrifugation was conducted at 4300 g, 30 min (in a swing bucket rotor). Broken plastids 357 were gently removed first from the top of the 30% Percoll ® layer. Intact plastids were harvested from the 358 70-30 layer and washed with 10 mL wash buffer (WB; IB without BSA and PVP40) by centrifugation at 359 3000 g, 10 min. The plastid pellet was washed again with 10 mL WB by centrifugation at 2000 g, 10 min. 360 The pellet was washed one final time with 1 mL WB by centrifugation at 1000 g, 10 min. From the plastid 361 pellets, crude lysate was formed by pulverizing them in presence of liquid nitrogen. Protein isolation buffer 362 (Invitrogen TM , Catalogue number FNN0011) was added to the powder and was incubated at 50 °C for 15 363 min for resuspension. The debris were pelleted at 10,000 g, 10 min and the supernatant was used for western 364 blotting. 365

Comparative organelle proteomics 421 

We clustered 56 Chloroplastida genomes and sorted them into organelle orthogroups as previously 422 described 20 . Briefly, we downloaded whole genome models for diverse Chloroplastida species, clustered 423 them into orthologous protein families (or orthogroups) using OrthoFinder version 2.5.4 117 and annotated a 424 subset of these orthogroups as plastid orthogroups (POGs) based on the presence of experimentally 425 validated plastid proteins from species where available: A. thaliana 36 , maize 35 , rice 35 ,426 

Physcomitrium patens 34 and C. reinhardtii 32,33 , as well as M. polymorpha (current study). Through this 427 approach, more than 99% of ca. 15k experimentally validated plastid proteins were efficiently clustered 428 into ca. 5k POGs (Table S3). The high efficiency is likely explained by the added experimental proteomes 429 and removed non-chloroplastidal species, where homologues are more diverged. 430
