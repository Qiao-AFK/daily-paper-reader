Abstract 9

10 

Despite their central role in metabolite exchange, lipid trafficking, and protein import, chloroplast 11 

outer envelope beta-barrel proteins lack a dedicated comprehensive sequence database 12 

spanning many plant proteomes. Here we present the database COB (chloroplast outer-13 

envelope beta-barrel), consisting of 16,586 beta-barrel sequences organized across ten protein 14 

categories and an uncharacterized group. COB was constructed using a machine learning 15 

classifier that identifies chloroplast beta-barrels based on features derived from evolutionary 16 

protein contact maps. Analysis of COB reveals that Streptophyta have more barrels overall and 17 

use a greater variety of solute transporters than Chlorophyta. Furthermore, we find considerable 18 

structural diversity across OEP categories, including variation in beta-strand count and a high 19 

prevalence of open barrel conformations not observed in bacterial outer membrane proteins. 20 

Structure predictions for Arabidopsis thaliana outer envelope proteins identified candidate hybrid 21 

barrel assemblies, with TOC159 family members emerging as universal interaction partners. We 22 

also report single-chain multi-barrel domain architectures in the chloroplast outer envelope, a 23 

topology previously described only in Gram-negative bacteria. Finally, we find chloroplast 24 

membrane barrels have more open topologies and shorter strands than bacterial membrane 25 

barrels. COB provides a comprehensive sequence resource for chloroplast outer envelope beta-26 

barrels and establishes a foundation for investigating the evolution, structure, and function of 27 

this essential protein in chloroplast. 28

Introduction 30 

31 

Chloroplasts are the organelles responsible for photosynthesis in plants and algae, converting 32 

light energy into chemical energy that sustains nearly all life on Earth [ 1]. Beyond their essential 33 

role in photosynthesis, they carry out a wide range of metabolic functions, including the 34 

synthesis of amino acids, fatty acids, and other molecules critical to plant growth and survival 35 

[2]. These vast and complex functions are rooted in the chloroplast's evolutionary origin as a 36 

once independent prokaryote, related to modern cyanobacteria, which formed an endosymbiotic 37 

relationship with an early eukaryotic host cell [ 3]. Mitochondria also arose through 38 

endosymbiosis, and the shared bacterial ancestry of these organelles is reflected in common 39 

structural features, including a double membrane system. Over the course of chloroplast 40 

evolution, the majority of the ancestral prokaryotic genome was transferred to the host nuclear 41 

genome, meaning that most chloroplast proteins must now be imported from the cytosol [ 3]. 42 

This created the need for dedicated protein import machinery at the chloroplast envelope, which 43 

is conducted by the translocon at the outer (TOC) and inner (TIC) envelope complexes [ 4, 5]. 44 

45 

The chloroplast outer envelope (OE) serves as the primary interface between the chloroplast 46 

and the rest of the cell, regulating the flow of ions, metabolites, and proteins into and out of the 47 

organelle [ 6-8 ]. Among the proteins embedded in this envelope are members of the beta-barrel 48 

protein family, a structural class found only in the outer membranes (OM) of bacteria, 49 

mitochondria, and chloroplasts [ 9]. The chloroplast outer envelope contains a set of defined 50 

outer envelope protein (OEP) categories that can be broadly grouped by function: solute 51

superfamily members (OEP80, SP2/P39, TOC75), and the TOC159 receptor family (TOC159, 53 

TOC132, TOC120, and TOC90). The four functional groups differ considerably in structure and 54 

function. 55 

56 

Solute transport: The solute transporter group mediates passage of a diverse set of molecules 57 

across the outer envelope. OEP21 is an ATP-regulated, anion-selective channel. It serves as 58 

the exit pore for triose phosphates (TPs), 3-phosphoglycerate, and inorganic phosphate (Pi), 59 

which are central intermediates of photosynthetic carbon fixation [ 10-12 ]. OEP24 serves a 60 

complementary role, though with broader substrate specificity, including TPs, dicarboxylic acids, 61 

amino acids, sugars, ATP, and Pi [ 13 , 14 ]. Although both channels participate in TP export, their 62 

relative abundance differs between plant species. OEP21 is prominent in C3 plants, while 63 

OEP24 is more abundant in C4 plants where its broader conductance and lower selectivity are 64 

hypothesized to better support higher metabolite rates [ 15 ]. In contrast, OEP37 is a cation-65 

selective channel whose substrate and primary role remain poorly defined [ 16 ], while OEP40 66 

selectively facilitates glucose transport and its phosphorylated derivatives [ 17 ]. The diversity 67 

shown across these channels reflects the complexity and specificity of metabolite exchange at 68 

the chloroplast OE. 69 

70 

Lipid transport: The lipid transporter group is represented by TGD4, which is a phosphatidic acid 71 

binding beta-barrel [ 18 , 19 ]. It is distantly related to the bacterial outer membrane protein LptD, 72 

which transports lipopolysaccharide across the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria [ 20 ]. 73 

Leaf glycerolipid composition in Arabidopsis is strongly enriched in chloroplast associated 74 

galactolipids, with mono- (MGDG) and digalactosyldiacylglycerol (DGDG) together accounting 75 

for about 60% of leaf glycerolipids [ 21 ]. Chloroplasts synthesize glycerolipids through two 76 

routes, a prokaryotic pathway retained entirely within the chloroplast, and a eukaryotic pathway 77 

in which fatty acids are exported to the ER for lipid assembly and subsequently reimported into 78 

the chloroplast. TGD4 functions in the eukaryotic pathway, where it binds phosphatidic acid at 79 

the outer envelope and facilitates reimport of ER-derived lipid precursors into the chloroplast. In 80 

Arabidopsis , a second LptD-family protein, LPTD1, is predicted to function as a lipid transporter 81 

with a particular role in glycerolipid remodeling under phosphate starvation [ 22 ]. 82 

83 

Protein movement: The Omp85 superfamily is a conserved family of outer membrane proteins. 84 

Bacterial BamA in this superfamily inserts beta-barrels into the bacterial outer membrane [ 23 ]. 85 

The chloroplast outer envelope contains multiple members of this superfamily, each serving 86 

distinct roles related to outer membrane biogenesis. OEP80 (TOC75-V) is thought to perform a 87 

function analogous to BamA [ 24 ]. Another Omp85 protein, SP2/P39, is evolutionarily related to 88 

the OEP80 family, but lacks the characteristic N-terminal POTRA domains. It functions as the 89 

core channel of the chloroplast-associated protein degradation complex (CHLORAD), mediating 90 

extraction of ubiquitinated OEPs for degradation [ 25 ]. Lastly, TOC75 is also a member of the 91 

Omp85 superfamily, and serves as the core channel of the TOC complex through which 92 

preproteins are translocated into the chloroplast [ 26 ]. 93 

94 

Receptors: The TOC159 family are the GTPase receptor components of the TOC complex, 95 

responsible for recognizing preproteins at the chloroplast surface. This is encoded by a small 96 

gene family with four homologous members, TOC159, TOC132, TOC120, and TOC90. Each 97 

shares a tripartite domain architecture consisting of an N-terminal acidic domain, a central 98 

GTPase domain, and a C-terminal membrane anchor domain that forms a beta-barrel 99 

embedded in the outer envelope. Although, the acidic domain is greatly truncated in TOC90 and 100 

sequence identity varies across paralogs [ 27-29 ]. Beyond their receptor function, the C-terminal 101 

beta-barrel domain also contributes structurally to the translocation pore itself. The TOC75 and 102

TOC159 family members come together to form a continuous hybrid beta-barrel pore through 103 

which preproteins are translocated in the TOC complex [ 30 , 31 ]. In this structure, strands from 104 

each protein contribute to a single, large continuous barrel. These paralogs assemble into 105 

compositionally distinct TOC complexes with TOC75. TOC159, and to a lesser extent TOC90, 106 

preferentially facilitate import of photosynthetic preproteins, while TOC132 and TOC120 107 

mediate import of housekeeping proteins [ 27-29 , 32 ]. Together, the TOC159 family and TOC75 108 

form the core machinery through which the vast majority of chloroplast preproteins are 109 

recognized and imported from the cytosol. 110 

111 

Previous efforts to identify and characterize chloroplast outer envelope beta-barrel proteins at a 112 

large scale have advanced through proteomic and computational approaches. Early mass 113 

spectrometry studies of spinach and Arabidopsis envelope fractions identified hundreds of 114 

envelope-associated proteins [ 33-35 ]. Successive proteomic studies refined and expanded 115 

these inventories through improved mass spectrometry sensitivity, cross-species comparison, 116 

and curated subplastidial localization databases [ 36-38 ]. Computational prediction of chloroplast 117 

beta-barrels was first attempted by adapting a bacterial scoring algorithm to the Arabidopsis 118 

genome, though this approach struggled to distinguish beta-barrels from soluble proteins and 119 

was limited to model organisms [ 39 , 40 ]. Curated reviews subsequently consolidated these 120 

datasets, with the total number of characterized chloroplast beta-barrel genes in Arabidopsis 121 

remaining approximately 18 (Supp. Table 1) [ 41 , 42 ]. 122 

123 

More recently, Roumia and co-authors used a large-scale computational approach to identify 124 

chloroplast beta-barrels across a range of plant species. Profile hidden Markov models 125 

(pHMMs) were developed for eukaryotic beta-barrel families of chloroplasts and mitochondria 126 

[43 ], which detected approximately 2,000 chloroplast beta-barrels across eukaryotic reference 127 

proteomes [ 44 ]. However, pHMM-based detection depends on sequence similarity to previously 128 

characterized families, which limits the ability to identify divergent homologs or families not yet 129 

recognized as beta-barrels. For example, the TOC159 receptor family was not recognized as 130 

forming a beta-barrel at the time and was therefore absent from these searches. Conversely, 131 

the OEP23 [ 45 ] channel was included as a beta-barrel, though structural predictions in this work 132 

indicate it does not adopt a beta-barrel fold, consistent with previous observations [ 46 ]. Despite 133 

these significant advances, no comprehensive database dedicated to chloroplast beta-barrel 134 

proteins across diverse plant proteomes currently exists. 135 

136 

Here, we describe a machine learning classifier that identifies beta-barrel proteins in the 137 

chloroplast based on features derived from evolutionary contact maps. We chose evolutionary 138 

contact maps over structure prediction methods for two reasons: lower computational costs and 139 

easier classification. While AlphaFold (AF) has allowed the generation of a vast number of 140 

structures, the majority of chloroplast outer envelope proteins across plant proteomes lack 141 

predicted structures, and large-scale 3D prediction for classification purposes remains 142 

computationally intensive [ 47 ]. The inherent structural properties of beta-barrels, including 143 

regular hydrogen bonding between adjacent antiparallel beta-strands, generates a characteristic 144 

evolutionary contact map that is reliably detectable. Creation and classification of this 2D 145 

representation is substantially more computationally efficient than full structure prediction, 146 

enabling classification across entire plant proteomes at a scale that would otherwise be 147 

impractical. AF structure prediction was then applied to validate high confidence classifier 148 

predictions rather than as a primary discovery tool. 149 

150 

Using our contact map-based classifier, we constructed COB, a comprehensive database of 151 

16,586 chloroplast outer envelope beta-barrel proteins organized across ten OEP categories. 152 

Through analysis of COB, we characterize the structural diversity across OEPs, examine the 153

Results 157 

158 

COB classifier 159 

160 

Our chloroplast outer envelope beta-barrel classifier is adapted from a previously developed 161 

algorithm that successfully predicted beta-barrel proteins in the bacterial outer membrane [ 48 ]. 162 

We hypothesized that our bacterial barrel classifier could be extended to the evolutionarily 163 

related chloroplast outer envelope. Briefly, the three steps of our classifier are: generate an 164 

evolutionary contact map, contact map feature extraction, classification. 165 

166 

Evolutionary contact maps were generated by RaptorX [ 49 ]. Beta-barrel proteins generate a 167 

distinctive pattern of diagonal contacts in their contact maps that are perpendicular to the self-168 

interaction diagonal. These slashes reflect regular hydrogen bonding between adjacent beta-169 

strands (Fig. 1A). Five features are extracted from the contact maps, including the number of 170 

beta-hairpins, average hairpin length, hairpin spacing standard deviation, a closing contact 171 

confidence score, and the protein sequence length (Fig. 1B-C). These features serve as input to 172 

a random forest classifier (Fig. 1D). The classifier was trained on a dataset we curated of 173 

predicted chloroplast beta-barrels and non-barrel proteins (1,537DS, See Methods). The model 174 

achieved a balanced accuracy of 85.1%, precision of 95.1%, and a Matthews Correlation 175 

Coefficient (MCC) of 75.8% when tested against a 10% hold-out set, demonstrating reliable 176 

discrimination between barrel and non-barrel proteins while minimizing false positives. 177

COB construction 179 

180 

Barrel identification 181 

182 

To build a comprehensive database of chloroplast outer envelope beta-barrels, we first applied 183 

the COB classifier to four representative Viridiplantae proteomes to generate a high-confidence 184 

initial set. We then expanded that set through database searching, and finally classified the 185 

sequences into ten OEP categories. 186 

187 

To identify chloroplast beta-barrels across Viridiplantae, we applied our COB classifier iteratively 188 

against four representative proteomes (Fig. 1E). These proteomes were selected to represent 189 

diversity within Viridiplantae. We used Arabidopsis thaliana (thale cress) [ 50 ] as a well-190 

characterized model organism, Triticum aestivum (bread wheat) [ 51 ] and Chlamydomonas 191 

reinhardtii (green algae) [ 52 ] as large NCBI proteomes for Streptophyta and Chlorophyta 192 

respectively, and Saccharum officinarum x Saccharum spontaneum hybrid cultivar R570 193 

(sugarcane) [ 53 ] as an extra-large proteome. Contact map generation of whole proteomes is a 194 

process that still requires large computational time, so we first determined that sequences 195 

sharing ≥40% sequence similarity receive the same COB prediction . We found ≥40% sequence 196 

similarity yields >97% same classification (barrel or not barrel, Supp. Fig. 1). This allows us to 197 

bypass map generation for any sequence similar to a sequence previously predicted. Each 198 

proteome was therefore clustered at 40% sequence identity using MMseqs2 [ 54 ], and the COB 199 

classifier was applied to cluster representatives rather than all sequences. 200 

201 

We began with T. aestivum, for which all contact maps for the representative sequences were 202 

generated as no prior COB predictions existed. The same workflow was then applied iteratively 203 

to C. reinhardtii, A. thaliana , and Saccharum hybrid. For each successive proteome, 204

As we searched whole proteomes, and outer membrane beta-barrels are found in both the 211 

chloroplast and mitochondrial outer membranes in eukaryotes, the COB classifier identified 212 

mitochondria annotated beta-barrels which were subsequently removed. 213 

214 

We discovered that some categories of chloroplast proteins (the families OEP40 and 215 

TOC159/132/120/90) were not identified by our COB algorithm. However, since they are known 216 

chloroplast barrels, we sought to include those in our database as well. Sequences from these 217 

families were therefore recovered using pHMM searches against all four proteomes. All 218 

candidate sequences from our workflow and the pHMM searches were manually validated using 219 

AlphaFold2 (AF2) structures to ensure membrane beta-barrel topology [ 47 ]. Together, the COB 220 

algorithm with the manual addition of OEP40 and TOC159/132/120/90 resulted in 344 unique 221 

sequences corresponding to 387 protein accessions, including 35 ( A. thaliana ), 9 ( C. 222 

reinhardtii ), 100 ( T. aestivum ), and 243 ( Saccharum hybrid) beta-barrels, yielding an initial high-223 

confidence set. 224 

225 

To expand this initial set, we BLAST searched the 344 unique sequences against the NCBI non-226 

redundant (nr) database restricted to Viridiplantae, retrieving 30,286 unique candidate protein 227 

accessions [ 55 , 56 ]. Of these, 20,244 sequences were sufficiently sequence similar (40%) to an 228 

existing barrel prediction to label them as barrels. 229 

230 

Categorizing barrels 231 

232 

To organize the 20,244 candidate barrel sequences into known chloroplast beta-barrel families, 233 

we classified them across OEP categories. The OEP categories are: OEP21, OEP24, OEP37, 234 

OEP40, OEP80, TOC75, TOC159, and TGD4. These categories group proteins by shared 235 

structural fold, and in some cases include homologs with distinct functions. OEP21 includes 236 

OEP21A and OEP21B, OEP24 includes OEP24A and OEP24B, TOC75 includes TOC75-III and 237 

TOC75-IV, TGD4 includes LPTD1, TOC159 includes TOC132, TOC120, and TOC90, and 238 

OEP80 (TOC75-V) includes SP2 (P39). 239 

240 

To categorize the proteins, we began with clustering the 20,244 sequences at 40% sequence 241 

identity using MMseqs2 [ 54 ]. Each cluster—composed of a representative sequence and its 242 

members—was assigned to the OEP category most represented across the NCBI annotations 243 

of all its members. This allowed us to classify large numbers of unannotated sequences by 244 

leveraging the consensus annotation of their cluster, rather than relying on individual sequence 245 

annotations alone. Clusters whose members could not reach a confident consensus assignment 246 

were placed in the Unclassified category. Clusters with predominantly mitochondrial beta-barrel 247 

annotations, including VDAC [ 57 ], Tom40 [ 58 ], or Sam50 [ 59 ], were removed from the dataset. 248 

After the initial sequence similarity clustering, some proteins in the Unclassified category had 249 

the fold of existing categories. Structural fold similarities can link proteins even after sequence 250 

similarity has been erased by evolution [ 60 ]. To better assign sequences remaining in the 251 

Unclassified category, we searched for fold similarities to representatives of each known OEP 252 

category using Foldseek [ 61 ]. Rather than classifying individual sequences, we applied a 253 

cluster-based approach using the MMseqs2 clusters and AlphaFold3 (AF3) structures (See 254 

Methods). Clusters whose members showed consensus structural similarity to mitochondrial 255

259 

Together, these annotation and structure-based classification steps produced COB, a database 260 

of 16,586 chloroplast beta-barrel proteins. Along with our previously known OEP categories, we 261 

identified two prominent clusters within the Unclassified group that were structurally distinct from 262 

any known OEP type. Although these clusters passed our mitochondrial filtering steps, we 263 

cannot confidently assign them to the chloroplast outer envelope. They were therefore 264 

designated Barrel of Unknown Localization 16 (BUL16) and BUL22, and named for their 265 

consensus closed strand counts of 16 and 22, respectively. In total, COB spans ten categories, 266 

including eight established OEP families, BUL16, BUL22, and an Unclassified category. 267 

268 

Previous work in OMPdb used pHMMs to identify 1,933 chloroplast beta-barrels across 269 

eukaryotic reference proteomes [ 44 ]. COB represents an over eight-fold increase relative to this 270 

dataset. To understand where this increase comes from, we examined how COB sequences 271 

relate to what was available to OMPdb at the time of their search (Supp. Fig. 2). Sequences 272 

from Viridiplantae RefSeq proteomes available in 2020 or earlier, approximating the proteome 273 

space accessible to OMPdb, account for 7.7% of the increase in COB. An additional 11.5% of 274 

COB comes from RefSeq proteomes released after 2020, reflecting growth in publicly available 275 

reference plant proteomes since that time. OMPdb restricted its search to reference proteomes, 276 

while we searched all available Viridiplantae sequences in the NCBI non-redundant database. 277 

The use of the non-redundant database accounts for 47.1% of the increase. We were also able 278 

to incorporate chloroplast beta-barrel types not present in OMPdb. The TOC159 receptor family 279 

was not recognized as forming beta-barrels at the time of prior searches and accounts for 280 

20.8% of the increase in COB. We additionally identified previously uncharacterized BUL16 and 281 

BUL22 categories, which contribute 12.9% of the increase in COB. 282

COB contains 16,586 beta-barrel proteins, organized into ten OEP categories and an 286 

Unclassified group (Fig. 2A). Each category has different structural features, including strand 287 

numbers, IMS and cytoplasmic domain architecture, and open or closed barrel, and yet they all 288 

share the N-terminal strand to the left of the C-terminal strand when orienting the 289 

intermembrane space (IMS) downward. The ten categories in COB are fairly equivalent in 290 

representation within the database, ranging from approximately 4-21% of the dataset (Fig. 2A). 291 

These categories can be further divided into the functional groups: solute transporters (OEP21, 292 

OEP24, OEP37, OEP40), lipid transporters (TGD4, LPTD1), Omp85 family members (OEP80, 293 

TOC75, SP2), the TOC159 receptor family (TOC159, TOC120, TOC132, TOC90), and unknown 294 

function (BUL16, BUL22) (Fig. 2A). Chloroplast OEPs constitute a very small percentage of 295 

reference plant proteomes, approximately 0.06% (Fig. 2B), and the OEP gene count scales with 296 

the size of the plant genome (Fig. 2C). Notably, the phylum Chlorophyta has fewer OEP barrels 297 

than Streptophyta, even after accounting for the relative differences in the number of protein-298 

coding genes. The distribution of OEP categories also differs among taxonomic classifications 299 

under Viridiplantae (Fig. 2D), demonstrating that not only does Chlorophyta have fewer OEP 300 

barrels than Streptophyta, but that it is especially deficient in the variety of solute transporters. 301 

While Streptophyta tends to have four separate types of solute transporters (OEP21, OEP24, 302 

OEP37, and OEP40), Chlorophyta often exclusively uses OEP21 with occasional instances of 303 

OEP24. Chlorophyta also lacks BUL22 or BUL16 (which is restricted to Magnoliopsida in 304 

Streptophyta) and has limited TGD4. 305

Chloroplast OEPs span across Viridiplantae 307 

308 

To characterize the abundance and distribution of chloroplast beta-barrel proteins within plant 309 

proteomes, we mapped COB sequences to their respective NCBI genome assemblies (the 310 

deposited genome and protein sequences for individual species or strains). This resulted in 861 311 

assemblies, of which 195 were RefSeq genomes and used for further analysis. Mapping protein 312 

accessions to their NCBI gene identifiers and collapsing redundant entries allowed us to gather 313 

the number of unique OEP genes per RefSeq genome. These barrel gene counts were divided 314 

by the total number of protein-coding genes reported for each genome. These results show that 315 

chloroplast beta-barrel genes account for approximately 0.06% of the total plant genome (Fig. 316 

2B). Additionally, the number of unique OEP genes detected per genome scales with overall 317 

proteome size (Fig. 2C, R 2 = 0.695), suggesting the number of OEP genes expands 318 

proportionally with genome size across Viridiplantae. The number of OEPs show a wide range 319 

from 3-80 unique OEP genes in a given RefSeq genome. This likely reflects the expansion of 320 

gene families through duplication events in larger plant genomes. This is illustrated by T. 321 

aestivum, a hexaploidy genome with three ancestral subgenomes [ 51 ] and the largest RefSeq 322 

genome in our dataset with 103,793 protein-coding genes. T. aestivum contains 67 unique OEP 323 

genes spanning all categories, which is consistent with the scaling of OEP content with genome 324 

size. 325 

326 

To examine OEP category composition across Viridiplantae, we analyzed the protein 327 

sequences in COB across all 861 proteomes. The distribution of individual OEP categories 328 

varies across the taxonomic classifications (Fig. 2D). Within Streptophyta, all ten OEP 329 

categories along with Unclassified barrels show a consistent presence across the represented 330 

taxonomic classes, demonstrating broad conservation throughout land plants and their close 331 

relatives. In contrast, proteomes within Chlorophyta show a more refined OEP set, with 332 

taxonomic classes primarily restricted to OEP21, OEP80, TOC75, TOC159, and sequences 333 

within the Unclassified category, while OEP24, OEP37, OEP40, TGD4, BUL16, and BUL22 are 334 

largely absent or rare. 335

domain, a feature typically associated with bacterial porins of comparable size. 366

The lipid transporter TGD4 is structurally related to the bacterial lipopolysaccharide transport 368

Lack of detectable beta-signal motif 414 

415 

A beta-signal motif has been proposed to reside in the last beta-strand of substrate proteins and 416 

is thought to be recognized by OEP80 during insertion into the outer envelope [ 24 ]. To 417 

investigate whether a conserved beta-signal is detectable across chloroplast OEPs, we 418 

searched for 6-10 residue sequence motifs using STREME and MEME [ 65 ]. These searches 419 

were performed on the non-redundant set of each OEP category, using both the full protein 420 

sequences and the last 50 residues separately. 421 

422 

While statistically significant hits were found for each category, none were consistently localized 423 

to the C-terminal beta-strand, and no motif spanned multiple OEP categories. This potentially 424 

reflects the high sequence similarity within OEP categories, even within our non-redundant sets 425 

at 70% sequence identity, rather than a definitive absence of a beta-signal. We also performed 426 

STREME analysis on all main OEP categories combined, excluding BUL and Unclassified 427 

sequences, at both 40% and 70% sequence similarity cutoffs. At 70% similarity, the analysis 428 

returned statistically significant motifs but with no consistency across individual motif searches, 429 

while at 40% similarity no significant motifs were detected. While there is biochemical evidence 430 

that demonstrates OEP80 interacting with the C-terminal beta-strands of substrate OEPs [ 24 ], it 431 

is also worth considering that our results could reflect biological diversity in substrate 432 

recognition. This could suggest that no universal C-terminal beta-signal exists across all OEP 433 

categories. Alternatively, multiple distinct motifs may be used across or within categories, or the 434 

beta-signal may be distributed elsewhere along the protein rather than in the last strand. A more 435 

sensitive analysis may require broader sequence diversity within each category or alternative 436 

approaches to motif detection. 437

Candidate Hybrid Beta-Barrel Structures 456 

457 

One possible explanation for the preponderance of open barrel structures is that these 458 

structures are homomeric or heteromeric dimers that form a barrel together, i.e. hybrid barrel 459 

structures. TOC75 is known to form a hybrid beta-barrel with members of the TOC159 receptor 460 

family within the TOC complex, with structural studies demonstrating an interaction specifically 461 

between TOC75 and TOC90 [ 28 , 30 , 31 ]. This evidence, along with the observed high 462 

prevalence of open barrel topologies, supports the possibility that hybrid barrel formation may 463 

extend more broadly across chloroplast OEP categories. To investigate this possibility, we 464 

retrieved all 28 unique A. thaliana beta-barrel sequences from COB and performed pairwise 465 

AF3 predictions for each sequence against every other sequence, including self-interactions 466 

(homodimers). Predictions with an intermediate interface predicted template modeling (TM) 467 

score ( ipTM ≥ 0.5 ) were retained, yielding 42 candidate interactions. Each was manually 468 

evaluated for hybrid barrel formation, and 31 were found to show hybrid barrel structures with 469 

contiguous interacting strands (Supp. Table 4). Notably, sequences within the TOC159 category 470 

were present in all 31 hybrid barrel interactions, indicating that TOC159 may serve as a 471 

universal interaction partner for hybrid barrel formation (Fig. 4A). These interactions involved not 472 

only TOC159 itself, but also other named proteins in the TOC159 category, supporting that 473 

hybrid barrel formation is potentially a property across the TOC159 family. The interacting 474 

partners included proteins in open barrel categories TOC75 and OEP80 as well as 475 

predominantly closed barrel categories OEP24 and TGD4 (Fig. 4B-D). Also, while lowering the 476 

ipTM threshold below 0.5 reduces confidence in predictions, we observed that OEP24 displayed 477 

similar universal hybrid-barrel behavior in the range of 0.4-0.5 ipTM, where it interacted with 478 

TOC159, OEP37, TOC75, and a sequence within the Unclassified category. While the TOC75-479 

TOC159 interaction is consistent with the known TOC complex structure as previously stated, 480 

the interactions involving OEP24, OEP80, and TGD4 represent putative novel hybrid barrel 481 

complexes not previously described. If these interactions are real, TOC159 proteins may have 482 

multiple partners that are engaged either permanently by different proteins or that the same 483 

TOC159 swaps to have a different partner at different times. 484

Comparison of chloroplast and bacteria sequences and contact maps 487 

488 

Throughout the development of COB, we observed features that differentiate chloroplast from 489 

bacteria beta-barrels at both the sequence and contact map level. To characterize these 490 

differences, we compared chloroplast OEPs in COB against bacterial OMPs from IsItABarrel. At 491 

the sequence level, chloroplast and bacterial beta-barrels show statistically significant 492 

differences in amino acid compositions (Fig. 5A). Chloroplast OEPs are enriched in charged 493 

residues, particularly positively charged amino acids, and show higher propensity for most non-494 

polar residues. However, the beta-branched amino acids tend to have lower propensity in 495 

chloroplast compared to bacteria. Additionally, chloroplast sequences generally show lower 496 

propensity for aromatic, polar, and small amino acids relative to bacterial OMPs. These 497 

compositional differences potentially reflect the distinct membrane environments, functions, GC-498 

content, and evolutionary histories between chloroplast and bacteria beta-barrels. 499 

500 

Chloroplast OEP categories also differ from bacterial OMPs in theoretical isoelectric point (pI) 501 

values (Fig. 5B). As these values are calculated from full protein sequences, the pI of individual 502 

categories reflects contributions from all domains, not only the barrel itself. This is most evident 503 

in the TOC159 category, which displays a notably lower pI relative to other OEP categories. 504 

This is consistent with its highly acidic N-terminal domain pulling the overall sequence pI down. 505 

The BUL16 and BUL22 categories display a tight bimodal pI distribution. This pattern is distinct 506

Discussion 555 

556 

In this study, we constructed COB, a comprehensive database dedicated to Chloroplast Outer 557 

envelope Beta-barrel proteins, consisting of 16,586 sequences across ten OEP categories and 558 

an uncharacterized group. COB was built using a machine learning classifier that identifies beta-559 

barrel proteins based on features derived from evolutionary contact maps. This coverage allows 560 

characterization of chloroplast beta-barrels at a larger scale than was previously possible. It 561 

allowed us to perform structural analysis across entire OEP categories, quantify the prevalence 562 

of open beta-barrels, characterize the abundance and distribution of OEPs across plant 563 

proteomes, and identify candidate barrel interactions. 564 

565 

Chloroplast beta-barrels are distinct from bacterial beta-barrels 566 

567 

Throughout the development of COB, we observed several differences between chloroplast and 568 

bacterial membrane beta-barrels. These differences are reflected in both the sequence and 569 

structural properties of the barrels, and informed the development of the COB classifier. 570 

571 

Chloroplast beta-barrels differ in amino acid preference compared to bacteria barrels 572 

573 

The distinct amino acid composition of chloroplast OEPs relative to bacterial OMPs may reflect 574 

differences in their respective membrane environments (Fig. 5A). In bacterial OMPs, aromatic 575 

residues form characteristic girdles at both membrane-water interfaces [ 9]. The reduced 576 

aromatic propensity in chloroplast OEPs may therefore reflect a reduced reliance on this 577 

anchoring mechanism. This is consistent with the fundamentally different lipid environment of 578 

the chloroplast OE. Unlike the bacterial OM, which is dominated by negatively charged 579 

lipopolysaccharide (LPS) in its outer leaflet, the chloroplast OE is enriched in galactolipids, 580 

which lack the charged headgroups. Additionally, we see an enrichment of positively charged 581 

residues in chloroplast OEPs (Fig. 5A), which may also be an effect of the lipid differences. In 582 

bacterial OMPs, a positive-outside rule has been described, where positively charged residues 583 

are enriched on the extracellular face of the barrel and interact with the negatively charged LPS 584 

in the outer leaflet [ 68 ]. The absence of LPS in the chloroplast OE suggests that the positive 585 

charge enrichment in chloroplast OEPs potentially serves a different purpose. Further, the 586 

differences in pI distributions across COB categories further supports that chloroplast OEPs are 587 

compositionally diverse in ways that may reflect their distinct domain architectures and function 588 

(Fig. 5B). The tight bimodal pI distribution observed in the BUL16 and BUL22 categories is 589 

notable, as it sets these categories apart from the remaining OEP categories. While the function 590 

of these groups remains unknown, this may indicate differences in their biology relative to the 591 

characterized OEP categories. 592 

593 

Chloroplast beta-barrels have shorter beta-strands than bacterial barrels 594 

595 

When evaluating the algorithm against chloroplast protein contact maps, we found that hairpin 596 

lengths were consistently shorter than those observed in bacterial OMP contact maps. To 597 

improve hairpin detection performance, we needed to lower the hairpin signal threshold and 598 

reduce the detection window size along the main diagonal of the contact map. To examine 599 

differences in hairpins more directly, we compared hairpin covariation lengths between the 40% 600 

clustered COB set and a non-redundant set of bacterial OMP contact maps from the IsItABarrel 601 

study (Fig. 5D) [ 48 ]. Chloroplast contact maps yielded an average hairpin covariation length of 602 

12.80 residues, compared to 15.05 residues in bacterial contact maps. These values are 603 

measured in contact map pixels, where each pixel represents a single residue. This hairpin 604 

covariation length in the contact map represents interactions between the contiguous beta-605 

> .CC-BY-ND 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted July 15, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.07.15.738633 doi: bioRxiv preprint

strands, therefore, this can be interpreted as differences in the strand lengths across beta-606 

barrels. This can generally be supported visually when comparing chloroplast OEP structural 607 

predictions with bacteria beta-barrels such as OmpA [ 69 ] or OmpT [ 70 ], that are generally taller 608 

and more elongated. While OmpA and OmpT are a small fraction of the types of beta-barrel 609 

structures found in bacteria, there is no comparable example to those beta-barrels 610 

characterized in chloroplast. 611 

612 

Limited sequence space of chloroplast OEPs compared to bacterial OMPs 613 

614 

Additionally, chloroplast contact maps showed more variability overall compared to those for 615 

bacterial OMPs. For instance, we observed two sequences that AF2 confidently predicted as 616 

membrane beta-barrels, yet their contact maps were essentially blank with no detectable 617 

features (Supp. Fig. 6). One potential explanation for this is the relatively lower number of 618 

sequenced homologs in chloroplast OEP families compared to bacterial OMP families. For 619 

instance, the bacterial phylum Proteobacteria (Pseudomonadota) (ncbitaxon:1224) has 620 

approximately 1.6 million genomes deposited in NCBI, whereas all of Viridiplantae (ncbitaxon: 621 

33090) has only around 8,000 (Accessed April 2026) [ 71 ]. RaptorX contact prediction relies on 622 

coevolutionary information from multiple sequence alignments, and prediction accuracy scales 623 

with the number of available homologs [ 49 ]. Therefore, this could have been a potential 624 

explanation for the observed inconsistencies in reliable, detectable features. The reduced 625 

number of sequence homologs also restricted the size of the positive training dataset for the 626 

classifier, which ultimately was validated through manual evaluation of AF2 predicted structures 627 

alongside contact map features. This also played into the choice of the random forest model, 628 

because it is a relatively simple model and well suited to small datasets. 629 

630 

Chloroplast OEPs display open-barrel structures and form candidate hybrid barrels 631 

632 

While bacteria beta-barrels consistently display closed barrel topologies in which the first and 633 

last beta-strands interact, chloroplast beta-barrels appear to display a mixture of open and 634 

closed forms. Categories OEP80, TOC75, and TOC159 were found to be almost exclusively 635 

open, while the remaining categories, OEP21, OEP24, OEP37, OEP40, TGD4, BUL16, and 636 

BUL22 showed varying frequencies of each (Table 1). As these analyses are based on AF3 637 

computational predictions, the high prevalence of open barrel conformations observed across 638 

OEP classes may reflect a systematic artifact of AF3 rather than true biological conformations. It 639 

is possible that some open barrel predictions may arise from incomplete sequences in the 640 

database, where a partial chain lacking one or more strands could prompt AF3 to predict an 641 

open conformation rather than a closed one. However, this pattern is consistent across all OEP 642 

classes and was not observed in analyses of bacterial beta-barrels. This mixture of open and 643 

closed conformations also manifested in the variation in the position and strength of the closing 644 

contact signal across chloroplast OEP contact maps compared to bacterial membrane barrels. 645 

This is supported by a direct comparison of closing contact prevalence between chloroplast 646 

OEPs and bacterial OMPs using the bacterial IsItABarrel closing contact algorithm [ 48 ]. 647 

Bacterial OMP contact maps contained a detectable closing contact signal in 82.0% of cases, 648 

compared to only 13.1% in chloroplast OEPs (Fig. 5C). However, because AF3 and RaptorX 649 

evolutionary contact maps both use MSAs, it’s unclear if these are both a feature of an MSA 650 

artifact, a feature of low information generating the MSA due to lack of proteomes, or a reality of 651 

protein structure in the chloroplast outer envelope. 652 

653 

If the preponderance of open barrel structures is not an MSA artifact, it is unclear if the high 654 

prevalence of open barrels across chloroplast OEPs is indicative of open barrels in the 655 

membrane or hybrid barrels in the membrane. It has been previously hypothesized that barrels 656

Strand count revisions 686 

687 

These analyses revealed upward revisions to strand counts across multiple OEP categories. 688 

For OEP24, prior estimates ranged from 7 to 14 strands, while our analysis predicts a 16-689 

stranded barrel [ 13 , 14 , 43 ]. OEP40 was previously predicted to form a 10-stranded barrel [ 17 ,690 

43 ], whereas our analysis consistently predicts 12 strands. Also, OEP37 was predicted to form a 691 

12 [ 39 ], 16 [ 43 ], or 18-stranded barrel [ 63 ], but we predict a 22-stranded barrel (Table 1). Earlier 692 

strand count predictions relied on sequence-based methods such as alternating hydrophobicity 693 

patterns and profile-based algorithms like PRED-TMBB2 [ 72 ]. These methods were developed 694 

based on bacterial OMPs, and not tuned for potential subtle differences between bacteria and 695 

chloroplast beta-barrels, such as shorter strand lengths. These prior strand count analyses have 696 

also largely been limited to individual sequences or small sets of proteins. By applying 697 

PolarBearal across larger sequence sets of each OEP category, we obtained consensus strand 698 

counts supported by hundreds of closed barrel predictions per family. 699

Chloroplast OEP composition varies across Viridiplantae 718 

719 

Chloroplast outer envelope beta-barrel genes only constitute approximately 0.06% of the total 720 

plant genome (Fig. 2B). It should also be noted that our percentages are calculated relative to 721 

the total plant genome, and normalizing to chloroplast-localized proteins only would likely give 722 

more comparable percentages to what has been seen in bacteria. To exemplify this 723 

comparison, we mapped the A. thaliana proteins in COB to their respective TAIR gene IDs [ 50 ], 724 

yielding 21 unique entries (Supp. Table 1). Given that ~3,000 proteins are imported into the A. 725 

thaliana chloroplast [ 77 ], OEPs represent ~0.7% of the chloroplast proteome. This is more 726 

comparable to the fraction in bacteria where beta-barrels account for approximately 0.27%-727 

6.79% of the genome [ 48 ]. However, nearly all transmembrane proteins in the outer membrane 728 

of bacteria take on a beta-barrel fold, and the chloroplast outer membrane contains proteins 729 

with both alpha-helical and beta-barrel transmembrane domains, so the two systems have 730 

different protein fold compositions [ 8, 78 ]. 731 

732 

Streptophyta and Chlorophyta represent the two major taxonomic lineages within Viridiplantae. 733 

Streptophyta encompass the charophyte algae and all land plants, while Chlorophyta comprise 734 

the morphologically diverse green algae [ 79 , 80 ]. This biological distinction is potentially 735 

reflected in their OEP category compositions (Fig. 2D). Streptophyta retain all ten OEP 736 

categories consistently across their proteomes. In contrast, Chlorophyta contains a more refined 737 

set, with solute transporters OEP24, OEP37, OEP40, and lipid transporter TGD4 largely absent 738 

or rare. The retention of OEP21, OEP80, TOC75, and TOC159 across both phyla is consistent 739 

with conservation of core outer envelope functions required for metabolite exchange (OEP21) 740 

[11 ], protein import (TOC75/TOC159) [ 30 ], and outer membrane biogenesis (OEP80/SP2) 741 

across Viridiplantae [ 24 , 25 ]. The absence of OEP24, OEP37, and OEP40 in Chlorophyta may 742 

reflect differences in metabolite trafficking between green algae and land plants. The relative 743 

abundance of OEPs already varies within land plants according to metabolite transport demand, 744 

as OEP24 and OEP37 are more abundant in C4 plants, while OEP21 predominates in C3 plants 745 

[15 ]. This pattern supports that OEPs are tuned to match specific metabolic demands for 746 

particular species. Chlorophyta exhibit distinct metabolic organization compared to land plants, 747 

including differences in carbon fixation, photorespiration, and central carbon metabolism [ 81 ,748 

82 ], which may alter the transport requirements across the outer envelope. In land plants, the 749 

demands of supplying energy across diverse tissues and cell types may have driven selection 750 

for these additional channels, whereas in Chlorophyta, OEP21 export may be sufficient. 751 

Additionally, the charophyte algae classes found in COB (Zygnematophyceae, 752 

Klebsormidiophyceae, and Charophyceae) also retain mostly all OEP categories (Fig. 2D). This 753 

pattern supports that diversification of the chloroplast outer envelope complement is an early 754 

evolutionary feature of Streptophyta, predating the emergence of land plants. 755 

756 

Furthermore, the absence of TGD4 across Chlorophyta may reflect a divergence in the 757 

machinery supporting ER-to-chloroplast lipid trafficking relative to plants. In land plants, lipid 758

Chloroplast OEPs display atypical multi-domain beta-barrel structures 772 

773 

Previously, proteins with multiple beta-barrel domains have been described in Gram-negative 774 

bacteria [ 66 ]. We detected structurally analogous architectures in the chloroplast outer 775 

envelope, which to our knowledge have not been previously reported (Fig. 6). The emergence 776 

of these structures in chloroplast may reflect similar evolutionary mechanisms to those 777 

proposed in bacteria, including tandem gene duplication and domain fusion [ 76 , 87 ]. However, 778 

some of the multi-barrel architectures observed in chloroplast may differ from those described in 779 

bacteria. The high proportion of open barrel topologies in chloroplast OEPs, as described 780 

above, may permit intra-chain hybrid-like arrangements not seen in bacteria. This is exemplified 781 

by KAM0881406.1, where two barrel domains within the same chain appear to interact through 782 

their open faces (Fig. 6B). This type of architecture has not been described in bacteria, where 783 

barrels are predominantly closed. This indicates that open barrel topology may introduce 784 

additional possibilities for how multi-domain barrel architectures can be arranged and potentially 785 

diversify in function. 786

Conclusions 788 

789 

We developed a contact map and machine learning-based classifier to identify chloroplast outer 790 

envelope beta-barrel proteins across Viridiplantae. The resulting database, COB, contains 791 

16,586 sequences corresponding to unique protein accessions in the NCBI non-redundant (nr) 792 

database. These sequences are organized into ten OEP categories, including OEP21, OEP24, 793 

OEP37, OEP40, OEP80, TOC75, TOC159, TGD4, BUL16, BUL22, and an Unclassified group. 794 

The categories display considerable structural diversity in strand number, domain architecture, 795 

and barrel topology, including a prevalence of open barrel conformations not observed in 796 

bacterial outer membrane beta-barrels. Consistent with this, we identified candidate hybrid 797 

barrel structures in A. thaliana , in which two open barrels interact to form a larger, continuous 798 

beta-barrel assembly. We also identified single-chain multi-domain barrel architectures in the 799 

chloroplast outer envelope, a topology previously described only in Gram-negative bacteria. The 800 

distribution of OEP categories differs between Streptophyta and Chlorophyta, suggesting 801 

distinct biological roles at the chloroplast outer envelope, particularly in metabolite transport and 802 

lipid trafficking. Collectively, COB provides a comprehensive sequence resource to support 803 

future characterization of chloroplast beta-barrels, and to further investigate the evolution and 804 

function of the chloroplast outer envelope across the plant kingdom. 805

COB classifier contact map feature extraction 836 

837 

Protein contact maps were generated using RaptorX [ 49 ]. Hairpins were detected by sliding a 838 

fixed sized window along the main diagonal of the contact map. Because beta-hairpins produce 839 

characteristic slashes perpendicular to the main diagonal, the sum of the anti-diagonal within 840 

each window position was used as a local signal for hairpin presence. Positions where this 841 

signal exceeded a threshold value were recorded as hairpin candidates. This is the same 842 

method described in the bacteria IsItABarrel algorithm for detecting hairpins, however with a 843 

lower threshold value of 9 [ 48 ]. 844 

845 

The algorithm then locates the start and end coordinates of each hairpin by searching outward 846 

from the recorded hairpin position on the main diagonal. The search moves in two directions, 847 

both perpendicular to the main diagonal and a small range along the diagonal to account for 848 

positional variation. It evaluates a local window for the presence or absence of an anti-diagonal 849 

(hairpin) signal. The start coordinate is recorded where a filled anti-diagonal is first detected, 850 

indicating the onset of strand pairing, and the end coordinate where the signal transitions to 851 

empty, indicating the termination of the strand pairing. 852 

853 

Average hairpin length was defined as the mean Euclidean distance between the start and end 854 

coordinates across all detected hairpins. Hairpin spacing standard deviation was defined as the 855 

standard deviation of distances between consecutive hairpin positions along the main diagonal. 856 

857 

The closing contact score captures the characteristic anti-diagonal signal produced by 858 

interactions between the first and last strands of a closed barrel. To detect this, the algorithm 859 

identifies intersection regions between each set of hairpin pairs at least 3 hairpins away from 860

each other. Within each candidate intersection, the anti-diagonal signal was evaluated by 861 

extracting a thick diagonal, formed by concatenating the main anti-diagonal and its two 862 

immediate neighbors. This thick diagonal was stepped incrementally in both directions along the 863 

main diagonal to account for slight variation in where the closing contact signal appears. The 864 

average value of this thick diagonal was computed at each offset position, and the maximum 865 

average across all offsets and all candidate intersections was returned as the closing contact 866 

score. 867 

868 

These features (number of hairpins, average hairpin length, hairpin spacing standard deviation, 869 

closing contact score, sequence length) were provided as input to the random forest classifier to 870 

produce a binary prediction of beta-barrel or non-beta-barrel. 871

Random forest classifier training and evaluation 873 

874 

The 1,537DS dataset was split into training (90%) and test (10%) sets using a homology-based 875 

strategy to minimize sequence similarity between sets. Sequences were clustered at 30% 876 

identity using MMseqs2, and clusters were assigned entirely to one set, ensuring no closely 877 

related sequences appeared in both. 878 

879 

A random forest classifier was trained using scikit-learn with a grid search over five 880 

hyperparameters, including bootstrap , class_weight, max_depth, min_samples_leaf, and 881 

min_samples_split [90 ]. Model performance was assessed using 7-fold cross-validation. Folds 882 

were constructed from the same 30% similarity clusters, ensuring each fold contained 883 

sequences dissimilar to those in all other folds. Models were first filtered by retaining only those 884 

with cross-validation standard deviations of ≤ 0.12 across precision, Matthews Correlation 885 

Coefficient (MCC), and balanced accuracy. From the remaining candidates, the model with the 886 

highest combined MCC and precision was selected. Final performance was evaluated on the 887 

10% held-out test set. 888

Foldseek structural classification 968 

969 

Clusters remaining in the Unclassified group following MMseqs2 annotation were subjected to 970 

structure-based classification using Foldseek [ 61 ]. To serve as Foldseek query structures, a 971 

representative AF3 structure was selected for each of the eight chloroplast OEP categories and 972 

three mitochondrial barrel proteins (VDAC, Sam50, and Tom40), chosen by visual inspection to 973 

confirm a confidently predicted, closed barrel structure. Mitochondrial representatives were 974 

included because sequence-based annotation alone may not have captured all mitochondrial 975 

proteins, and structural similarity provides an additional filter. For each MMseqs2 cluster defined 976 

as Unclassified, sequences were reduced to 70% sequence similarity using EMBOSS Stretcher 977 

data (See Redundancy reduction algorithm in Methods), and the remaining sequences were 978 

collected as classification targets. Each of the eleven representative query structures were 979 

searched against all the non-redundant Unclassified sequences using Foldseek easy-search 980 

with TMalign global alignment. For each target sequence, only query hits that met the minimum 981 

thresholds of 0.6 average TMscore and 0.7 query and target coverage were retained. 982

Classification of each non-redundant Unclassified MMseqs2 cluster proceeded in three steps. 984 

First, a consensus hit was required, meaning a cluster was eligible for classification only if all 985 

non-redundant members with a significant Foldseek hit agreed on a single query category. 986 

Second, the required proportion of cluster members with a matching hit varied depending on 987 

whether successful PolarBearal strand count predictions were available for non-redundant 988 

sequences in that cluster. For clusters with at least one strand count prediction, the strand count 989 

was required to match the expected consensus closed strand count for the assigned category 990 

(Table 1), and at least 10% of the non-redundant cluster members were required to have a hit to 991 

that query. For clusters lacking any strand count predictions, the threshold was raised to require 992 

at least 50% of members to have a hit to the consensus query. Third, a special case was 993 

applied for VDAC and Tom40. Because these two mitochondrial proteins share a 19-stranded 994 

topology and frequently produced overlapping Foldseek hits, clusters receiving exactly two 995 

unique query hits corresponding to both VDAC and Tom40 were classified as mitochondrial 996 

under the same strand count and hit rate thresholds as described. Clusters that did not meet 997 

these criteria remained uncharacterized and were retained in the Unclassified category. 998

bacterial OMPs, closing contact presence data was taken directly from the IsItABarrel study for 1053 

the 101,152 representative contact maps [ 48 ]. To gather hairpin covariation lengths, contact 1054 

map features were extracted using the COB classifier for the 733 COB non-redundant contact 1055 

maps and the non-redundant set of 101,152 bacterial OMP contact maps. To obtain the hairpin 1056 

covariation lengths in residues, the hairpin length was first calculated as previously described 1057 

using the Euclidean distance between hairpin start and end coordinates. Since these 1058 

coordinates are oriented exactly diagonally on the contact map, dividing this value by √2 1059 

converts it to the number of pixels along the hairpin axis. This pixel count represents the hairpin 1060
