Abstract 12 

The pyrenoid is an organelle found in nearly all algae that concentrates CO ₂ around 13 

Rubisco to enhance photosynthetic carbon fixation. Engineering a functional pyrenoid into 14 

C₃ plants is a promising route to improve plant photosynthetic performance and yields. 15 

However, progress has been limited by the lack of a plant platform for rapidly testing 16 

candidate components. Here, we engineered N. benthamiana to make its chloroplast 17 

Rubisco holoenzyme compatible with C. reinhardtii pyrenoid proteins. Disruption of native 18 

N. benthamiana RBCS genes and complementation with the C. reinhardtii RBCS2 19 

generated a Rubisco-recombinant background where transient expression of the Rubisco 20 

linker protein EPYC1 was sufficient to drive the formation of Rubisco–EPYC1 21 

condensates. Co-expression with the algal pyrenoid kinase KEY1 shifted the multi-22 

condensate state toward a single condensate per chloroplast by altering EPYC1 23 

phosphorylation in planta , recapitulating C. reinhardtii pyrenoid regulation. Together, 24 

these results establish engineered N. benthamiana as a tractable platform for rapidly 25 

characterizing candidate pyrenoid proteins and provide a step toward reconstructing 26 

pyrenoid architecture and regulation in plants. 27

Introduction 28 

29 

Photosynthetic carbon assimilation in many crop plants is limited by the performance of 30 

the CO 2-fixing enzyme Rubisco (Ort et al., 2015; Bouvier et al., 2024). In C ₃ plants, 31 

Rubisco's poor performance arises from low CO ₂ availability at the site of carboxylation, 32 

resulting in decreased catalytic rate and frequent wasteful oxygenation reactions with 33 

Rubisco's alternative substrate, O ₂ (Tcherkez et al., 2006). To enhance Rubisco's 34 

performance, some organisms have evolved CO ₂-concentrating mechanisms (CCMs) 35 

that elevate CO ₂ concentration around Rubisco (Raven et al., 2008; Sage et al., 2012). 36 

There is currently significant interest in understanding how these CCMs work and 37 

engineering them into plants to enhance crop performance (McGrath and Long, 2014; 38 

Hennacy and Jonikas, 2020; Fei et al., 2022). A functional CCM has the potential to 39 

enhance crop biomass, nitrogen use efficiency, water-use efficiency, and temperature 40 

resilience (Vogan and Sage, 2011; Long et al., 2015; Ainsworth and Long, 2021; 41 

Bernacchi et al., 2025). 42 

43 

The green algal pyrenoid-based CCM is particularly promising for engineering because 44 

of its unique characteristics. Unlike C ₄ photosynthesis, which typically requires extensive 45 

modifications to leaf anatomy (Kajala et al., 2011), the algal CCM operates at the level of 46 

a single cell, simplifying the engineering required to reconstitute it in plants. Additionally, 47 

individual pyrenoid components are natively encoded in the nuclear genome, and most 48 

localize correctly when expressed in plants without modification; others have been shown 49 

to require only the addition of a known transit peptide for correct targeting (Atkinson et 50 

al., 2016). Among pyrenoid-based CCMs, the one from the green alga Chlamydomonas 51 

reinhardtii is the most promising target for engineering into plants, both because it is the 52 

best-understood and because C. reinhardtii is relatively phylogenetically close to land 53 

plants, increasing the likelihood that its components will be compatible with plant systems 54 

(Leliaert et al., 2012; Atkinson et al., 2016; He et al., 2023). 55 

56 

At its core, the C. reinhardtii pyrenoid contains a liquid-like biomolecular condensate, 57 

called the matrix (Freeman Rosenzweig et al., 2017), that houses densely packed 58

through phase separation driven by multivalent interactions between Rubisco and the 60 

intrinsically disordered linker protein EPYC1 (Mackinder et al., 2016; Wunder et al., 2018; 61 

He et al., 2020; Barrett et al., 2021). The matrix is traversed by specialized thylakoid 62

2020; He et al., 2023; Fig. 1a). 66 

67 

Considerable progress has been made toward reconstituting elements of the C. 68 

reinhardtii pyrenoid in plants, including the assembly of Rubisco condensates (Atkinson 69 

et al., 2020), partial reconstruction of matrix-traversing membranes (Hennacy et al., 70 

2024), and recruitment of starch granules to the matrix (Atkinson et al., 2024). However, 71 

progress remains limited by an incomplete understanding of which components are 72 

required for minimal functional pyrenoid formation (Adler et al., 2022). 73 

74 

A major challenge in identifying these missing factors has been the relatively slow pace 75 

of testing candidates for assembly, localization, and regulation in an engineered system 76 

(Hennacy and Jonikas, 2020). Arabidopsis thaliana has served as a primary platform for 77 

stable expression of algal CCM components, but its limited amenability to transient 78 

expression limits effective iteration times to 4-6 months, imposed by transgenic plant 79 

generation time (Atkinson et al., 2017; Atkinson et al., 2020; Hennacy et al., 2024). 80 

Transgenerational protein silencing has further constrained systematic pyrenoid protein 81 

testing. 82 

83 

To address these bottlenecks of pyrenoid engineering in plants, here we sought to 84 

develop a Rubisco-recombinant system for testing pyrenoid components in the model 85 

plant Nicotiana benthamiana . In contrast to Arabidopsis, N. benthamiana has been widely 86 

used for transient protein expression, including in reconstitution of complex metabolic 87 

pathways (Lau and Sattely, 2015; Klein and Sattely, 2017; Bally et al., 2018; Dudley et 88 

al., 2022), and provides an effective system for testing protein localization in planta 89

limited use in pyrenoid reconstitution because the endogenous Rubisco small subunit 91 

(RBCS) in N. benthamiana lacks the C. reinhardtii -specific residues required for binding 92 

of many pyrenoid proteins (Meyer et al., 2012; He et al., 2020; Meyer et al., 2020). The 93 

unmodified plant was therefore not expected to support pyrenoid assembly. Replacing 94 

endogenous RBCS with an algal counterpart is not straightforward: flowering plants 95 

encode large, multicopy RBCS gene families, and most loci must be disrupted to 96 

suppress competition from incompatible endogenous subunits. 97 

98 

In the present work, we produce a pyrenoid-compatible N. benthamiana by disrupting the 99 

endogenous RBCS genes and expressing their C. reinhardtii counterpart (Fig. 1b). We 100 

demonstrate the value of this system by reconstituting condensate formation using rapid, 101 

transient expression of EPYC1. We further use this system to establish that the number 102 

of pyrenoid condensates can be regulated in plants by expressing the pyrenoid kinase 103 

KEY1 (Fig. 1c). These results establish engineered N. benthamiana as a plant platform 104 

for rapid screening of candidate pyrenoid proteins and testing their roles in pyrenoid 105 

assembly, function, and regulation. 106

CRISPR/Cas9-mediated genome editing disrupts RBCS accumulation in N. 108

As a first step to establish a pyrenoid reconstitution system in an N. benthamiana , we 111 

needed remove and replace endogenous NbRBCS with the C. reinhardtii version to 112 

enable binding of C. reinhardtii pyrenoid components (Meyer et al., 2020). We queried 113 

the N. benthamiana genome assembly (Wang et al., 2024), accessed via Phytozome, 114 

https://phytozome-next.jgi.doe.gov with known A. thaliana RBCS protein sequences and 115 

identified eight photosynthetic RBCS loci, distributed across chromosomes 3, 17, 18, and 116 

19. The eight loci fell into two structural groups based on exon organization: Group I (four 117 

exons, three introns) and Group II (three exons, two introns) (Supplementary Fig. 1a), 118 

consistent with the two RBCS subfamilies previously described in Nicotianeae (Jamet et 119 

al., 1991) . One additional locus (Nbe11g19750) showed markedly lower sequence 120 

identity to the photosynthetic family (~53% identity) and was excluded from targeting on 121 

the basis of its high sequence similarity to the trichome-specific NtRBCS-T protein 122 

described in tobacco (Laterre et al., 2017). We designed four sgRNAs to target conserved 123 

RBCS regions, ensuring that each of the eight loci was covered by at least two 124 

independent guides (Supplementary Fig. 1b). 125 

126 

We initially attempted Agrobacterium -mediated stable transformation with CRISPR-Cas9 127 

overexpression constructs carrying single guide RNAs (sgRNAs), analogously to a recent 128 

approach used to disrupt RBCS in the related diploid species Nicotiana sylvestris (da 129 

Silva et al., 2026), but this approach yielded severely chlorotic T0 regenerants that failed 130 

to root and did not survive tissue culture even on sucrose-supplemented medium, 131 

precluding downstream complementation (Supplementary Fig. 2). We therefore 132 

employed a tissue culture-independent approach. 133

Two months after infiltration with mobile sgRNAs, plants developed distinct white patches 142

stems (Fig. 2c). The green stem likely retained functional Rubisco and supported growth 148

Transient expression of EPYC1 reconstitutes EPYC1-Rubisco matrix-like 205 

condensates in Rubisco-recombinant N. benthamiana 206 

With a viable Rubisco-recombinant plant in hand, we next asked whether it supports algal 207 

pyrenoid matrix assembly. Matrix assembly in C. reinhardtii relies on multivalent 208 

interactions between Rubisco and the intrinsically disordered linker protein EPYC1 (He 209 

et al., 2020). To test whether transient EPYC1 expression is sufficient to promote 210 

condensate formation in our Rubisco-recombinant N. benthamiana , we transiently 211 

expressed EPYC1 as a GFP fusion under a constitutive promoter. Since it was previously 212 

shown that EPYC1 requires a chloroplast targeting signal for localization in A. thaliana 213 

(Atkinson et al., 2020) we also fused the A. thaliana RBCS1a chloroplast transit peptide 214 

to the N-terminus of EPYC1 (Fig. 4a). 215 

216 

We observed that transient expression of EPYC1-GFP in the Nbrbcs;CrRBCS2 217 

background consistently led to the formation of multiple discrete condensates of varying 218 

sizes within chloroplasts of all the three Rubisco-recombinant lines (Fig. 4b, 219 

Supplementary Fig. 3a). The condensates were also observed by transmission electron 220 

microscopy as distinct electron-dense structures within chloroplasts (Fig. 4c). In contrast, 221 

transient expression of EPYC1-GFP in wild-type N. benthamiana only resulted in diffuse 222 

stromal localization without detectable condensate formation (Fig. 4b), indicating that 223 

condensate formation depends on the presence of the C. reinhardtii Rubisco small 224 

subunit, as previously observed in A. thaliana (Atkinson et al., 2020). EPYC1 expression 225 

also did not cause visible leaf phenotypes such as chlorosis or necrosis in either wild-226

To rule out the possibility that the multiple condensates we observed in the 230

The pyrenoid matrix forms by the co-condensation of Rubisco and EPYC1 (Mackinder et 239 

al., 2016; Wunder et al., 2018), so to confirm that the condensates we observed actually 240 

represent a reconstituted matrix, we transiently expressed CrRBCS2-mCherry in stable 241 

EPYC1 -GFP -expressing Nbrbcs;CrRBCS2 lines. CrRBCS2-mCherry signal co-localized 242 

with EPYC1-GFP condensates (Fig. 4d), demonstrating that Rubisco is enriched within 243 

the condensates. Together with the strict dependence of condensate formation on the 244 

CrRBCS2-containing background, these data establish that EPYC1 has phase-separated 245 

with CrRBCS2-containing Rubisco, reconstituting in N. benthamiana the core 246 

compositional features of the C. reinhardtii pyrenoid matrix. 247

Pyrenoid kinase KEY1 promotes single condensate formation in N. benthamiana 249

In C. reinhardtii, the pyrenoid kinase KEY1 is required to maintain a single pyrenoid per 251 

chloroplast (He et al., 2026). KEY1 is thought to promote exchange of Rubisco and 252 

EPYC1 with the stroma, which results in dissolution of smaller condensates and growth 253 

of a single larger one through Ostwald ripening. The loss of KEY1 results in multiple 254 

pyrenoid condensates, and such mutants show impaired growth under ambient CO ₂255

chloroplast-targeted KEY1 with EPYC1-GFP markedly shifted condensate organization. 269 

Whereas EPYC1-GFP typically formed multiple condensates per chloroplast in the 270 

Nbrbcs;CrRBCS2-2 background, co-expressing it with KEY1 predominantly produced a 271 

single condensate per chloroplast (Fig. 5b,c). Quantifying the condensates per 272 

chloroplast revealed a significant decrease in condensate number upon KEY1 co-273

Discussion 292 

293 

Here we report the generation of a Rubisco-recombinant N. benthamiana platform that 294 

enables rapid functional characterization of pyrenoid components in a plant context. By 295 

replacing endogenous NbRBCS loci with the C. reinhardtii counterpart, we obtained 296 

plants expressing a hybrid plant-algal Rubisco holoenzyme compatible with algal 297 

pyrenoid proteins. Transient expression of EPYC1 in this background drove Rubisco-298 

EPYC1 condensate formation, and co-expression of the pyrenoid kinase KEY1 299 

consolidated multiple condensates into a single condensate per chloroplast, apparently 300 

through phosphorylation of EPYC1 in planta .301 

302 

The generation of the Rubisco-recombinant line required overcoming a fundamental 303 

obstacle for a photosynthetic organism: Rubisco is essential for photoautotrophic growth. 304 

In N. benthamiana , complete disruption of the eight NbRBCS loci through conventional 305 

stable transformation produced T0 regenerants that failed to survive tissue culture, 306 

precluding the stable transformation needed for complementation. We therefore used 307 

mobile sgRNA delivery via agroinfiltration into Cas9-expressing plants (Ellison et al., 308 

2020) which produced edited plants with white and green mosaic leaves. Regeneration 309 

from white leaf tissue yielded, serendipitously, a single chimeric regenerant in which a 310 

green stem likely sustained adjacent Rubisco-null white tissue through intercellular 311 

metabolite exchange (Fig. 2c), an arrangement functionally analogous to deliberate 312 

grafting strategies used to sustain photosynthetically-impaired plants (Kanevski and 313 

Maliga, 1994). We used white tissue from this plant directly as a source for stable 314 

transformation with CrRBCS2 , bypassing the lethality that would otherwise preclude 315 

recovery of the complemented line. The exploitation of a naturally occurring chimeric 316 

regenerant as a transformation intermediary has not, to our knowledge, been previously 317

We demonstrate here that this Rubisco-recombinant background enabled condensate 321 

formation upon transient expression of GFP-tagged EPYC1, making our engineered N. 322 

benthamiana the second plant species after A. thaliana in which Rubisco condensates 323 

have been reconstituted by stable EPYC1 expression (Atkinson et al., 2020). This result 324 

suggests that Rubisco condensate formation can be robustly achieved across plant 325 

species when appropriate molecular interfaces are engineered (He et al., 2020; Meyer et 326 

al., 2020). However, in N. benthamiana , the expression of GFP-tagged EPYC1 327 

consistently resulted in multiple condensates per chloroplast (Fig. 4, Supplementary Fig. 328 

3a) rather than the single punctum typically observed in Arabidopsis (Atkinson et al., 329 

2020). This phenotype was observed across both transient and stable expression 330 

contexts, suggesting that formation of multiple condensates represents a default outcome 331 

of EPYC1-driven phase separation in N. benthamiana chloroplasts (Supplementary Fig. 332 

3b,c). One possible explanation for this difference is that the less-complete replacement 333 

of RBCS in Arabidopsis may result in weaker EPYC1-Rubisco interactions, which could 334 

promote Ostwald ripening toward a single dominant condensate. Alternatively, an 335 

endogenous chloroplast kinase in A. thaliana may phosphorylate EPYC1 and promote 336 

condensate ripening. This latter idea raises the more general possibility that different host 337 

plant species may influence pyrenoid assembly in different ways that will need to be 338 

accounted for in engineering efforts. 339 

340 

In algae, loss of the pyrenoid-associated kinase KEY1 is linked to the formation of multiple 341 

pyrenoid condensates, and KEY1 has been shown to control pyrenoid number through 342 

phosphorylation of EPYC1 (He et al., 2026). In the engineered N. benthamiana system, 343 

co-expression of KEY1 with EPYC1 reorganized the multi-condensate state into a 344 

predominantly single condensate per chloroplast, and KEY1 expression was associated 345 

with a reproducible shift in EPYC1 mobility on Phos-tag gels, consistent with the KEY1-346 

dependent phosphorylation of EPYC1 in planta (Fig. 5) . KEY1 expression alone did not 347 

induce condensate formation, supporting the idea that its role in this system is regulatory 348

modularity and rapid component iteration. Transient expression of candidate pyrenoid 358 

proteins can be completed from cloning to results within 14 days, enabling rapid 359 

assessment of localization and interaction with other pyrenoid components. Proteins 360 

required for pyrenoid-traversing membrane formation (Hennacy et al., 2024), carbonic 361 

anhydrase localization to the pyrenoid lumen, and starch sheath assembly remain either 362 

unidentified or poorly characterized in a heterologous context and represent immediate 363 

targets for testing in this system. Stable EPYC1-GFP lines, in which Rubisco-EPYC1 364 

condensates are constitutively present, provide a defined starting point for introducing 365 

additional candidate proteins by transient expression. This creates a branching 366 

framework where each stable expression milestone enables the next round of rapid 367 

screening. Together with the condensate reconstitution and regulatory results presented 368 

here, this stepwise approach, moving from transient validation to stable integration, lays 369 

the conceptual and experimental foundation for the systematic reconstitution of a370 

functional pyrenoid in plants and, ultimately, in agriculturally relevant crop species. 371

Photosynthetic measurements 446 

Leaf gas exchange was measured using a LI-6800 Portable Photosynthesis System (LI-447 

COR Biosciences) on leaves 3–5 of 2 month-old plants grown in a greenhouse under 448 

natural light conditions. Unless stated otherwise, chamber conditions were set to: T xchg 449 

23.4°C, reference CO ₂ 440 μmol mol −1 , PPFD 1500 μmol m −2 s−1 , and airflow 500 μmol 450 

s−1 , corresponding to an average VPD of 1.89 ± 0.27 kPa. Fully expanded leaves were 451 

clamped in the chamber, then net CO ₂ assimilation ( A), stomatal conductance ( gs), and 452 

intercellular CO ₂ (Ci) were recorded at steady state. To establish steady-state stomatal 453 

conductance, leaves were first acclimated to a CO ₂ level of 400 μmol mol −1 . CO ₂ was 454 

then stepped down to 50 μmol mol −1 and held until stability was reached, allowing stomata 455 

and Rubisco activation state to equilibrate before measurements began. These 456 

acclimation points were excluded from the final A/Ci curve. A/Ci curves were generated 457 

by stepping CO ₂ from 50 to 1500 μmol mol −1 across 12 setpoints. 458 

To determine the CO ₂ compensation points, a linear regression was fit to the first four 459 

points on each A/Ci curve. The compensation point (Γ) was the Ci calculated from each 460 

regression by setting A equal to zero. The maximum rate of Rubisco carboxylation ( Vcmax )461 

and maximum electron transport rate ( Jmax ) were estimated by fitting A/Ci curves to the 462 

Farquhar–von Caemmerer–Berry (FvCB) model of C3 photosynthesis (Farquhar et al., 463
