ABSTRACT 25 

Within the algal carbon -assimilating organelle, the pyrenoid, specialized traversing 26 

membranes perform the essential function of deliver ing concentrated CO 2 to Rubisco . In 27 

Chlamydomonas reinhardtii , these membranes consist of peripheral cylindrical tubules 28 

that connect to a central reticulated region . However, due to resolution limitations , the 29 

spatial distribution of the ir structural and functional proteins has remain ed unclear . Here, 30 

we achieve an ~11 -fold improvement in resolution by combin ing ultrastructure 31 

expansion microscopy with super -resolution instantaneous structured illumination 32 

microscopy , revealing protein localizations and condition -dependent remodeling of 33 

the se membranes . At ai r levels of CO 2, the tubule -initiating protein SAGA1 forms narrow 34 

rings at the pyrenoid edge, the tubule -extending protein MITH1 surrounds the peripheral 35 

tubules , and the putative transporter BST4 surrounds tubules more centrally, suggesting 36 

that the cylindrical tubules contain multiple distinct protein domains. The CO 2-delivering 37 

carbonic anhydrase CAH3 localizes to the inner face of the central reticulated region ,38 

suggesting that this region is specialized for CO 2 delivery . CAH3 remains in the 39 

reticulated region at high CO 2, suggesting that the cell maintains a minimal CO 2-delivery 40 

apparatus even when dispensable. Finally, at high CO 2, cylindrical tubule diameter 41 

narrows , and MITH1 relocalizes throughout the pyrenoid -traversing membrane network. 42 

Together, our study elucidates sub -pyrenoid protein organization and CO 2-dependent 43 

reorganization. 44

INTRODUCTION 49 

The pyrenoid, an organelle found in the chloroplasts of many algal species, is 50 

responsible for approximately one -third of global photosynthetic CO ₂ fixation 1–4. To 51 

date, the pyrenoid of the genetically tractable model alga Chlamydomonas reinhardtii 52 

(Chlamydomonas hereafter) remains the best molecularly and morphologically 53 

characterized 5,6 . It consists of the matrix, a liquid -like condensate 7 of the CO 2-fixing 54 

enzyme Rubisco clustered by the linker protein EPYC1 (Cre10.g436550) 4,8,9 ; a network 55 

of thylakoid membrane -derived traversing membranes 10 –12 that deliver concentrated 56 

CO 2 to Rubisco 13 –15 ; and a surrounding starch sheath that likely serves as a diffusion 57 

barrier to prevent CO 2 loss (Figure 1a -b)16,17 . The CO 2-delivery function of the pyrenoid -58 

traversing membranes is achieved by the thylakoid carbonic anhydrase CAH3 59 

(Cre09.g415700) housed in the lumen 16,18 –21 . CAH3 convert s abundant luminal HCO 3-60 

to concentrated CO 2, driven by the low pH environment of the lumen 15,22 . This 61 

concentrated CO 2 then diffuse s out of the pyrenoid -traversing membranes to enhance 62 

the enzymatic activity of the Rubisco in the matrix . There is considerable interest in 63 

engineering algal pyrenoids into crops, as mathematical modeling suggests that this 64 

could improve yields and increase both water and nitrogen use efficiency 16,23 –25 .65 

While the pyrenoid matrix has been structurally and functionally well -characterized, 66 

understanding the proteins involved in the biogenesis and shaping of the pyrenoid -67 

traversing membr anes is the next frontier in pyrenoid biology. At air levels of CO 2 (0.04% 68 

v/v), the pyrenoid -traversing membranes are defined by two distinct morphological 69

transmission electron microscopy (TEM) 12,26 and more recently by cryo -electron 71 

tomography (cryoET) 10 . Near the periphery of the pyrenoid, flat thylakoid sheets merge 72 

into cylindrical tubules ~100 nm in diameter , which radially enter the matrix. Near the 73 

cent er of the pyrenoid, the cylindrical tubules narrow in diameter, merge with each other 74 

via three -way junctions , and form the reticulated region (Figure 1a -b). Why the 75 

pyrenoid -traversing membranes exhibit these two distinct morphological domains 76 

remains a major open question in the field. 77 

Understanding the potential functions of the pyrenoid -traversing membranes' 78 

domains requires precisely mapping membrane protein localization, as knowledge of a 79 

protein’s subdomain localization is critical to understanding its potential function within 80 

the pyrenoid -traversing membranes . In addition to the CO 2-delivering CAH3, s everal 81 

other key players that function in or on the pyrenoid -traversing membranes have been 82 

identified, including the putative transmembrane transporter BST4 (Cre06.g261750) , the 83 

tubule -initiating protein SAGA1 (Cre11.g467712) , and the tubule -extending protein 84 

MITH1 (Cre06.g259100) . However, we currently lack a clear picture of their precise 85 

spatial organization. Furthermore, while traversing -membrane morphology has been 86 

well -studied at air levels of CO 2, the pyrenoid is known to be smaller under high CO 287 

conditions 4,27 , and whether traversing -membrane morphology and protein localization 88 

undergo remodeling under high CO 2 conditions also remains uncharacterized. 89 

A major obstacle to mapping the precise spatial organization of key factors has been 90 

that t he small diameter and tight packing of the traversing membranes make them 91 

challenging to resolve by conventional confocal microscopy. Super -resolution light 92

To overcome these technical challenges to visualizing protein localization within the 98 

pyrenoid -traversing membranes , here we combine Ultrastructure Expansion Microscopy 99 

(U -ExM) with super -resolution instantaneous SIM (iSIM) 31 . U -ExM has been used 100 

previously with Chlamydomonas cells to visualize protein localization and cellular 101 

morphology, with a focus on the cytoskeleton and flagellar basal bodies 32 –34 . We adapt 102 

the Chlamydomonas U -ExM protocol to study pyrenoid -traversing membrane protein 103

known pyrenoid membrane proteins, notably suggesting a role for the central reticulated 107 

region in localizing the essential CO 2-delivering carbonic anhydrase CAH3. Finally, we 108 

discover that the morphology and protein distribution of the pyrenoid -traversing 109 

membranes respond to changes in environmental CO 2 concentrations. Together, these 110 

discoveries advance the understanding of the molecular architecture of the pyrenoid. 111

To achieve an effective 10 - to 12 -fold increase in fluorescence imaging resolution of 116 

pyrenoid ultrastructure, we combined an enhanced expansion microscopy protocol with 117 

instantaneous structured illumination microscopy (iSIM). In an expansion microscopy 118 

workflow, cells are embedded in a swellable hydrogel , the structural molecules are 119 

denatured to make the sample mechanically homogen ous , and then the sample is 120 

physically expanded, which improves the resolution achievable by light microscopy 121 

(Figure 1c). We first optimized the existing Chlamydomonas U -ExM workflow 32 to 122 

increase the expansion factor from four -fold to approximately six -fold (see Methods). 123 

Next, w e imaged the expanded cells with iSIM to gain an additional two -fold increase in 124 

lateral and axial resolution 31 . We visualized the global protein distribution of the 125 

Chlamydomonas cells using a fluorescently conjugated NHS -ester 35 –37 , a compound 126 

that reacts with primary amines on proteins. As observed previously in cryo -ExM studies 127 

of Chlamydomonas cells 33,34 , the protein -dense pyrenoid appears as a densely stained 128 

sphere at its canonical position at the base of the cup -shaped chloroplast in both 129 

unexpanded (Figure 1d -e, S1a -b) and expanded cells (Figure 1f -g, S1c -d). By 130 

quantifying pyrenoid diameter before and after expansion, we found that our modified 131 

gel chemistry yielded an average expansion factor of ~6 -fold (Figure 1h), compared to 132 

the ~4 -fold achieved using the original U -ExM protocol. In addition, this enhanced 133 

expansion factor did not compromise pyrenoid circularity (Figure 1i). Thus, by combining 134 

modified U -ExM gel chemistry with iSIM imaging, we achieved an effective lateral 135 

resolution of ~24 nm without distorting the native shape of the pyrenoid. 136

The pyrenoid -traversing membrane proteins MITH1 and BST4 localize to spatially 185 

distinct subdomains 186 

Existing light microscopy studies of pyrenoid proteins have been able to distinguish 187 

between a protein localized to the matrix, membranes , and starch sheath , but have had 188 

very limited ability to determine the localization of a protein within a sub domain of the 189 

pyrenoid -traversing membranes 5,6,29,38,39 . By combining immunofluorescence with NHS -190 

ester based pan -protein staining, we determined the distribution of two known pyrenoid 191 

tubule -localized proteins with highly specific antibodies : the tubule -extending protein 192 

MITH1 29 and the putative transmembrane transporter BST4 (also called RBMP1) 39 .193 

Previously, both MITH1 and BST4 have been localized to the pyrenoid -traversing 194 

membranes via their colocalization with the chlorophyll -positive thylakoid signal in the 195 

pyrenoid 29,39 . We reasoned that any potential subdomain -specific localization within the 196 

network could reflect functional specialization of the proteins, the subdomain, or both. 197 

Given the approximately circular cross -section of the pyrenoid, we quantitatively 198 

compared the radial distributions of MITH1 and BST4 as a function of the distance from 199 

the centroid of the pyrenoid. We found that while the pan -protein staining was evenly 200 

distributed across the axis of the pyrenoid, MITH1 signal was largely restricted to the 201 

cylindrical tubules at the pyrenoid periphery (Figure 3a -b, S3a, Movie S3). The 202 

distribution of the transmembrane BST4 38,39 occupied an intermediate radial distribution, 203 

being less enriched in the reticulated region at the center of the pyrenoid and not 204 

extending as far peripherally as MITH1 (Figure 3e -f, S3b, Movie S3). Our previous work 205 

has show n that the mith1 mutant fails to extend tubules through the pyrenoid matrix 29 .206

MITH1 may be involved in initiating and maintaining the outermost tubules , while the 208

Next, we examined protein localization across the axis of individual tubules using the 210 

tubule edge -associated increased pan -protein staining density as a fiducial marker. On 211 

the peripheral tubules, we found that MITH1 signal overlapped with the increased pan -212 

protein stain density (Figure 3c -d) , while the transmembrane BST4 signal localized 213 

internally relative to the peaks of pan -protein stain . Using transmembrane BST4 as a 214 

proxy for the tubule membrane, our results suggest that the antibody epitope -containing 215 

C-terminus of MITH1 is localized distally from the tubule membrane (Figure 3g -h). This 216

recent structural evidence suggesting that MITH1 forms an elongated coiled -coil that 219 

extends away from the membrane and into the matrix 40 .220 

Together, combining immunofluorescence with U-ExM + iSIM revealed that MITH1 is 221 

positioned peripheral to BST4 at both the whole -pyrenoid and sub -tubular scales, 222 

demonstrating the utility of this approach for resolving spatial protein organization in the 223 

pyrenoid. The distinct localizations of MITH1 and BST4 also suggest that the cylindrical 224 

tubules possess at least two distinct protein subdomains that are not morphologically 225 

apparent by electron microscopy. 226

The thylakoid carbonic anhydrase CAH3 is essential for pyrenoid function 14,18,20,21 and is 229 

thought to facilitate the release of concentrated CO 2 from the pyrenoid tubules by 230 

converting HCO 3- to CO 215,22 . Prior immunogold EM with anti -CAH3 antibodies observed 231 

immunogold particles associated largely with the pyrenoid tubules, with fewer particles 232 

associated with the surrounding thylakoid sheets 41 –43 . More recent 233 

immunofluorescence -based localization observed CAH3 to be localized almost 234 

exclusively to the pyrenoid tubules 29 . However, the specific localization of CAH3 within 235 

the tubule network has remained unexplored due to limitations in the sensitivity and 236 

spatial resolution of the existing techniques. 237 

We used immunofluorescence in combination with U -ExM + iSIM to examine the 238 

localization of CAH3 in the pyrenoid. Like the prior immunodetection studies, we also 239 

observed the vast majority of CAH3 signal in the pyrenoid tubules. However, in contrast 240 

to the localization of MITH1 and BST4, we observed that CAH3 signal was localized 241 

almost exclusively near the centroid of the pyrenoid in the region corresponding to the 242 

narrower tubules of the reticulated region (Figure 3i -j, S3c, Movie S3). Th ese results 243 

indicate that CAH3 localizes primarily to the reticulated region of the pyrenoid -traversing 244 

membranes. 245

Tubule diameter increases in response to low CO 2 concentrations 287 

The pyrenoid is a dynamic organelle whose morphology is known to respond to 288 

environmental CO 2 concentrations. Prior work has documented that after shifting 289 

Chlamydomonas cells from growth in high to low CO 2, the pyrenoid matrix condensate 290 

grows 4,27 and the starch sheath grows to surround the pyrenoid 48 . However, how the 291 

morphology of the pyrenoid -traversing membranes changes in response to a transition 292 

from high to low CO 2 has , to our knowledge, not been described. 293 

To study changes in tubule morphology, we cultured wild -type cells at high CO 2294 

conditions (HC; 3% CO 2 v/v) and transferred them to grow under low CO 2 conditions (air 295 

level , 0.04% CO 2 v/v ) for 1 (LC 1h), 3 (LC 3h) and 16 (LC 16h) hours before fixation and 296

pyrenoid -traversing membranes during the transition from high to low CO 2 conditions. 319

MITH1 relocalizes in response to CO 2 availability 334 

We next examined the distribution of MITH1 in the pyrenoids of cells grown at high 335 

CO 2 and transitioned to low CO 2. Unlike CAH3 and BST4, a t high CO 2, MITH1 signal 336 

was found throughout the pyrenoid -traversing membranes , including near the center of 337 

the pyrenoid. With increasing time at low CO 2, MITH1 was depleted from the central 338 

tubules and adopted its strictly peripheral localization described earlier (Figure 6c). Our 339 

results establish that MITH1 localization changes during the transition from high to low 340

DISCUSSION 343 

Leveraging the high -resolution imaging provided by U -ExM + iSIM and protein 344 

localizations by immunofluorescence allowed us to contribute new insights into sub -345 

pyrenoid protein localizations and how they change from high to low CO 2 growth 346 

conditions (Figure 7). 347 

We found that the key carbonic anhydrase CAH3 localizes specifically to the central 348 

reticulated region , suggesting that the reticulated region is specialized for CO 2 release. 349 

If the reticulated region is responsible for CO 2 release, t his suggests that the cylindrical 350 

tubules that connect the reticulated region to the surrounding thylakoids may serve as 351 

conduits for transport of ions, substrates, and products. 352 

Our findings suggest that CAH3 localizes to the surface of the tubule membrane; 353 

however, the mechanistic basis of CAH3’s association with the membrane is unknown. 354 

One possibility is that CAH3 can independently associate with membrane s as t he CAH3 355 

dimer has a predicted hydrophobic region that has been hypothesized to allow it to 356 

interact with membranes 51,52 ; however , this has not been tested. Alternatively, CAH3 357 

could interact with an unknown membrane -localized targeting factor that localizes CAH3 358 

to the membrane .359 

Given that the lumen of the pyrenoid tubule network is continuous with that of the 360 

chloroplast thylakoids 10 , it is also currently unknown how CAH3 remains restricted to the 361 

pyrenoid tubules under all growth conditions. CAH3 , independently or via a binding 362 

partner, might sense the unique morphology or curvature of the reticulated tubules and 363 

preferentially localize to them . Phosphorylation of CAH3 in response to low CO 2364 

conditions has also been proposed as a possible mechanism for its localization to the 365

understanding the mechanism of CAH3 localization is a crucial step in the engineering 369

While luminal CAH3 and transmembrane BST4 retained their radial localization s371 

independent of CO 2 concentrations, we were surprised to find that the radial localization 372 

of MITH1 changed from high to low CO 2. MITH1 was uniformly distributed throughout 373 

the tubule network at high CO 2 and became increasingly peripheral during the transition 374 

to low CO 2. We recently showed that MITH1 can bind both membranes and Rubisco, 375 

likely serving to link the pyrenoid tubule and matrix compartments 40 . As the proportion of 376 

pyrenoid -localized Rubisco decreases at high CO 24, the spatial distribution of MITH1 377 

may be driven by the availability and radial extent of Rubisco. Thus , at high CO 2, MITH1 378 

may ingress deeper into the pyrenoid -traversing tubule network to prevent unsatisfied 379 

Rubisco -binding sites and maintain connection with both membranes and matrix .380 

Previous electron microscopy -based studies had documented the persistence of the 381 

pyrenoid -traversing membranes in cells cultured at high CO 248 . We found that the 382 

cylindrical tubule diameter is smaller in cells grown at high CO 2 than in those grown at 383 

low CO 2. We additionally found that the increase in tubule diameter lags behind the 384 

increase in matrix diameter during the high -to -low CO 2 growth transition . Th ese 385 

observations suggest that the pyrenoid -traversing membranes are slower to remodel in 386 

response to changing CO 2 conditions and might explain why they persist in growth 387 

conditions where the function of the pyrenoid is not required. 388

MATERIALS AND METHODS 641

Chlamydomonas culture conditions 643 

The strains used in this study can be found in Table S1. All strains were maintained on 644 

1.5% agar containing Tris -acetate phosphate (TAP) medium with revised trace minerals 645 

at room temperature (~22 °C) under very low light (<10 μmol photons ⋅m-2⋅s-1). For liquid 646 

culture for U -ExM experiments, strains were inoculated in ~10 mL of Tris phosphate 647 

(TP) medium in 125 mL flasks capped with sterile vented tissue sealing film (MSE 648 

supplies, 30 mm vented membrane, 0.2 –0.3 μm pore size) while stirring at 180 rpm for 649 

aeration under continuous cool white light at ~150 μmol photons ⋅m-2⋅s-1. For growth in 650 

high CO 2 conditions, strains were cultured in 3% (v/v) CO 2 for two days before 651 

harvesting. For growth in low CO 2 conditions, strains were first cultured at high CO 2 as 652 

described and then diluted into fresh TP medium before moving to a different incubator 653 

with the same light, temperature and stirring parameters except low CO 2 levels (0.04% 654 

v/v) for the indicated amount of time before harvesting. Prior to harvesting, cell counts 655 

were measured by a Countess II Automated Cell Counter (Life Technologies). 656

Ultrastructure expansion microscopy 658 

Ultrastructure expansion microscopy was performed as described previously 32 with 659 

minor changes. Briefly, 10 mL of Chlamydomonas cells were harvested at the specified 660 

timepoint by centrifugation at 600 x g for five minutes and then resuspended in 1 mL of 661 

fresh TP medium. 200 μL of each cell suspension was pipetted onto an 18 mm poly -D-662 

lysine coated coverslip (Neuvitro) and 100 μL of the same suspension was pipetted onto 663 

a 12 mm poly -D-lysine coated coverslip to be used as the un -expanded control. All 664

coverslips were placed in a 12 -well plate and cells were allowed to adhere for ten 665 

minutes under ~120 μmol photons ⋅m-2⋅s-1 white light. 666

paraformaldehyde, 0.1% glutaraldehyde in 1X PBS) was added per coverslip and the 668 

plate was placed on ice for slow fixation over 60 minutes. Next, the fixative solution was 669 

removed and replaced with 1 mL of ice -cold 100 mM glycine solution to quench the 670 

fixation for five minutes on ice. The glycine solution was removed, and the coverslips 671 

were washed three time s in ice -cold 1X PBS. Finally, 1 mL of ice -cold anchoring 672 

solution (2% acrylamide, 1.4% formaldehyde in 100 mM bicarbonate buffer) was added 673 

to each coverslip and the samples were allowed to anchor at 4 °C overnight with no 674 

shaking. 675
