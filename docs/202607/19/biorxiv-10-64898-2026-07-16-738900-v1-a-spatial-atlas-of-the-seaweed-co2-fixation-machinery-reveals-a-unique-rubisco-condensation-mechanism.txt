# Summary 38 

Seaweeds (macroalgae) are important primary producers that sustain food webs in coastal 39 

ecosystems. Most algae accelerate inorganic carbon assimilation by actively concentrating 40 

CO 2 in a Rubisco-rich specialized organelle called the pyrenoid. However, the molecular 41 

composition of this pathway is unknown in seaweeds. Here, we investigated the intracellular 42 

localization of 160 proteins associated with CO 2 acquisition in the green seaweed Ulva (Sea 43 

lettuce). We assign 68 proteins to different pyrenoid subdomains and identify a consensus 44 

Ulva Rubisco binding motif revealing the molecular logic of the Ulva pyrenoid. We reveal 45 

Seaweed Ulva Pyrenoid Assembly 1 (SUPA1) as the core pyrenoid assembly factor. We show 46 

that Rubisco condensation is driven by a unique mechanism: the helical folding of SUPA1 47 

motifs upon Rubisco binding, combined with steric hindrance that halves the available 48 

Rubisco binding sites from eight to four. Our data gives an unprecedented sub-cellular spatial 49 

understanding on seaweed carbon fixation and provides insights into the evolution of this 50 

important pathway in the global carbon cycle. 51

# Introduction 52 

Seaweeds are a colorful, biodiverse and polyphyletic group of organisms. There are over 53 

12,000 currently described seaweed species, most of which are spread across three 54 

evolutionarily distinct groups: the brown, green and red macroalgae. These groups are 55 

named after their dominant pigmentations and each group has independently evolved 56 

multicellularity 1,2 so that all three groups contain species across a remarkable diversity of 57 

sizes and shapes. 3 The seaweeds as a whole are critical primary producers and ecosystem 58 

engineers in coastal areas worldwide, and both the area and productivity of their biomes are 59 

comparable to those of the Amazon rainforest. 4 Their ability to rapidly transform captured 60 

inorganic carbon (Ci) into growth potential is exemplified by frequent and extensive blooms 61 

that can lead to hugely damaging economic and environmental impacts. 5 Conversely, 62 

harvested or cultivated seaweed biomass is often processed as food, feed or fertilizer, and 63 

holds promise as a source of pharmaceuticals and cosmetics. 6

64 

Despite this economic, evolutionary and environmental importance, the assimilation of Ci by 65 

seaweeds remains relatively poorly understood at the molecular level. Although the fixation 66 

of CO 2 by Rubisco is conserved in all photosynthetic organisms, this carboxylation reaction 67 

is catalytically slow 7 and Rubisco has a poor selectivity for CO 2 over O 2, which can reduce 68 

net carbon fixation by up to 50%. 8 Mechanisms have therefore evolved to concentrate CO 269 

in the vicinity of Rubisco to favor the carboxylation reaction 9,10 and accelerate biomass 70 

accumulation. 71 

In the majority of marine algae, this Ci concentration takes place in a specialized chloroplast 72 

organelle called the pyrenoid, and pyrenoid-based CO 2-concentrating mechanisms 73 

(pCCM) 9,11 have been estimated to contribute ~30% to global photosynthetic carbon 74 

capture. 12 Pyrenoids are biomolecular condensates consisting of Rubisco and other proteins 75 

that mediate organelle formation, structure and maintenance. 13,14 Under limiting CO 276 

conditions, the pCCM functions by actively assimilating bicarbonate (HCO 3-) from (sea)water 77 

and ultimately dehydrating it to CO 2 by a carbonic anhydrase (CA) enzyme close to Rubisco. 15 

78 

The molecular pCCM components have been characterized to varying extents in a small 79 

number of microalgae and hornworts. 16–20 In the freshwater microalga Chlamydomonas 80 

reinhardtii (Chlamydomonas hereafter), for example, high-throughput protein-tagging 81 

experiments have provided extensive insights into the pCCM. By mapping molecular 82 

components to subcellular regions, these studies revealed a spatially organized pathway in 83 

which HCO 3- is transported across the plasma membrane, chloroplast envelope and 84 

thylakoid membranes to the pyrenoid composed of multiple functionally-specialized 85 

subdomains. 16,21,22 These studies, and localization of components in other algal systems, 86 

underscore that the precise subcellular localization of core pCCM transporters, enzymes and 87 

structural components is critical for pathway function. 16,18,20,21 

88 

However, data indicate that pyrenoids have convergently evolved across different algal 89 

clades and may therefore display structural and molecular diversity. 11,17–19 For example, in the 90 

core chlorophyte lineages of the Chlorophyceae and Trebouxiophyceae, multivalent 91 

disordered linker proteins have independently evolved to bind and condense Rubisco to form 92 

the pyrenoid matrix. 12,19 The interaction of linkers and other structural proteins with Rubisco 93

95 

Consequently, determining the spatial organization of the pCCM is a crucial first step to 96 

understanding how the principles of Ci assimilation work in larger, multicellular, marine algae. 97 

The scale of such experiments necessitates bona fide genetic model systems that, to date, 98 

have only been available in the unicellular freshwater chlorophyte Chlamydomonas 16,21 and, 99 

to a lesser extent, the unicellular diatom Thalassiosira pseudonana .23 Although recent 100 

advances in macroalgal functional biology have yielded genetic models for Ectocarpus ,101 

Neopyropia and Ulva that support stable transformation and targeted mutagenesis 24–29 , high-102 

throughput gene manipulation remains to be demonstrated in these models. 30,31 

103 

In this study, we rectify this omission to provide a spatial protein blueprint of the carbon 104 

capture pathway in a species from the green seaweed genus Ulva (Sea lettuce), a much-105 

studied exemplar for marine macroalgal photosynthesis. 32–34 Ulva is a globally distributed 106 

genus that occurs primarily in marine, but also in brackish and even freshwater habitats. 35–37 

107 

Ulva species are morphologically plastic but typically comprise of a single layer of 108 

photosynthesizing blade cells and a small holdfast for attachment ( Fig. 1A ). 38–40 Ulva species 109 

feature the world’s highest photosynthetic rates per area 4,33 and opportunistic Ulva blooms, 110 

known as green tides, are increasingly perturbing coastal ecosystems worldwide, with 111 

extremely detrimental environmental and economic impacts 5. More positively, Ulva biomass 112 

is also exploited in aquaculture applications. 41,42 Either way, Ulva ’s prolific growth is 113 

associated with an efficient conversion of Ci and nutrients into biomass 43,44 , driven by a highly 114 

efficient pCCM that has been confirmed by physiological studies 34,45–47 , but whose molecular 115 

components remain unexplored and undescribed. 116 

In this study we leverage our recently developed genetic tools in Ulva compressa/mutabilis 117 

(Ulva hereafter) to fluorescently tag 201 proteins associated with the pCCM, providing an 118 

unprecedented first spatial understanding of a seaweed pCCM. Our microscopy data reveal 119 

core components of the carbon fixation pathway, identifying 68 proteins that localize to 120 

subdomains of the pyrenoid, of which 15 are exclusively localized to this condensate. We 121 

identify the core pyrenoid assembly protein, which we name Seaweed Ulva Pyrenoid 122 

Assembly protein 1 (SUPA1). Using a combination of in vitro , in vivo and structural studies 123 

we show that SUPA1 is required for Rubisco condensation to form the pyrenoid by binding 124 

to both the small and large Rubisco subunits at four locations per holoenzyme. Our study is 125 

a first demonstration that high-throughput screens are possible in macroalgae and provides 126 

a strong foundation for the molecular understanding of carbon capture in an ecologically and 127 

economically important organism. 128

# Results + discussion 129 

Ulva has a distinct pyrenoid ultrastructure 130 

In agreement with previous studies 48–50 TEM microscopy showed that Ulva cells contain a 131 

large chloroplast that occupies ~35% of the cell area ( Fig. S1 ). Within each chloroplast is a 132 

single pyrenoid encapsulated by a starch sheath that was typically formed from two, but 133 

sometimes three, starch plates. In all TEMs with a central pyrenoid mid-section we observed 134 

a single traversing membrane continuous with the thylakoid network. This suggests that the 135 

pyrenoid traversing thylakoids (PTTs) in Ulva consist of a single thylakoid membrane sheet 136 

(Fig. 1B-C; Fig. S1 ), in contrast to the chlorophyte Chlamydomonas , which contains multiple 137 

tubular PTTs fusing at the center of the pyrenoid 51 , and the trebouxiophyte Chlorella , which 138 

has a double-layered sheet-like PTT. 52 Additionally, Ulva displays a large thylakoid structural 139 

change adjacent to the PTT entry sites on both sides of the pyrenoid. In these regions, 140 

multiple (20-45, n=11) stromal thylakoids converge, transitioning from typical flat ordered 141 

stacks into a disordered network where they appear to fuse into a single swollen thylakoid 142 

that then enters the pyrenoid matrix ( Fig. 1B-C; Fig. S1 ). We term these regions thylakoid 143 

convergent zones and propose that they enable the connection of a large volume of the 144 

thylakoid lumen to the PTT. 145 

Identification of Ulva pCCM-related genes 146 

We reasoned that an orthology-based approach would be the quickest way to identify an 147 

initial set of proteins putatively involved in the Ulva pCCM. We therefore turned to the well-148 

characterized pCCM of the single-celled model green alga Chlamydomonas 16,21,53 , which 149 

belongs to the Chlorophyceae, a class that diverged from the Ulvophyceae in the 150 

Mesoproterozoic or early Neoproterozoic (1300-800 Mya). 54,55 Components of the 151 

Chlamydomonas pCCM can be broadly split into three categories: 1) Ci transport uptake 152 

components that shuttle CO 2/HCO 3- from the surrounding environment to the pyrenoid ( Fig. 153 

1D ); 2) pyrenoid structural components; and 3) regulatory components that control pCCM 154 

induction and pyrenoid assembly. In addition to these core components, many further 155 

Chlamydomonas genes have been associated with its pCCM in large-scale gene-tagging, 156 

transcriptomic and/or proteomic studies, and through detailed biochemical characterization. 157 

However, the exact function of many of these genes remains to be demonstrated. 13,14 Recent 158 

advances in the understanding of pCCM components in the Trebouxiophyceae (split from 159 

Ulva >850 Mya 19,55 ) and hornworts (split from Ulva >1,1 Gya 20,56,57 ) suggested that some Ulva 160 

proteins responsible for Ci uptake and/or pyrenoid assembly would be conserved with 161 

Chlamydomonas while others would not. 162 

We therefore narrowed down a list of 610 Chlamydomonas pCCM-related genes that 163 

includes known Chlamydomonas pCCM components, pyrenoid-related genes and genes 164 

that are differentially expressed under low CO 2 conditions 16 by excluding genes with a less 165 

well-established link to the pCCM pathway (“other” category in Mackinder et al. 16 ). We 166 

supplemented this list with organelle markers (chloroplast, ER, Golgi, mitochondria, nucleus, 167 

peroxisomes, plasma membrane and vacuole) 16 and more recently identified pyrenoid-168 

localized proteins in Chlamydomonas .21,53 From the resulting 343 pCCM-associated 169 

Chlamydomonas genes we determined orthologous gene relationships using the PLAZA 170

Large-scale fluorescent tagging assigns 160 proteins to 34 subcellular locations 178 

We next aimed to leverage stable transformation 25,59 to get a comprehensive and spatially-179 

resolved view of the molecular components of the Ulva pCCM that determine its architecture 180 

and function ( Fig. 1B-D ). Accordingly, we fluorescently tagged the gDNA sequence from 201 181 

of 309 genes (65%; Table S2 ), with cloning success dependent on a combination of factors 182 

including gene size, GC content and FPKM ( Fig. S2A-C ). All genes were fused to a C-terminal 183 

Yellow Fluorescent Protein (YFP) sequence via Golden Gate assembly in an expression 184 

vector, under control of a constitutive promoter and the nopaline synthase terminator (NOST) 185 

(Fig. 2A ). Expression vectors were stably transformed into Ulva gametes . After three weeks 186 

of selection, 3-29 (average 10) independent stably transformed individuals were imaged per 187 

construct ( Fig. 2A , Table S2 ). 188 

This allowed unambiguous intracellular localization of 160 tagged proteins ( Fig. S3 ). Except 189 

for two proteins, we obtained localization data in at least two, on average six, independent 190 

transformants ( Table S2 ). Our success rate of 80% of constructs yielding protein localization 191 

data is high compared to 33-49% in Chlamydomonas in similar screens. 16,21 This indicates 192 

that transgene silencing 60 may be less of a challenge in Ulva , further supported by 57% of 193 

transformants per construct expressing the fusion protein compared to <15% in 194 

Chlamydomonas (Table S2; Fig. S2A-H ). 61 To better understand endogenous abundances of 195 

pCCM-related proteins and to determine if they impacted localization success rates, we 196 

integrated available RNA expression data 62 and generated whole cell proteomic datasets of 197 

Ulva (Table S2; Table S4 ). Overall, localization success is not correlated with RNA expression 198 

level, unlike Chlamydomonas 16 , but is with higher protein abundance ( Fig. S2A-L ). 199 

Since this is the first protein-tagging study of this scale in a seaweed, we used the localization 200 

of marker proteins to guide the classification of unknown proteins. Marker proteins were 201 

selected based on established organelle and sub-organelle localization from orthology or 202 

previously generated localization data. 25,39,59,63 Guided by a decision tree that integrated 203 

marker protein data and image features, we classified the 160 localized proteins into 34 204 

subcellular regions ( Fig. 2B; Table S2 ). Despite our reference gene set and success in 205 

transgene expression, artefacts or incomplete observations cannot be excluded since we 206 

used a constitutive promoter and we imaged at a single developmental time point. For 207 

example, flagella are absent during vegetative development in Ulva so localization to this 208 

cellular structure was not evaluated. For some proteins a specific sub-cytosol or secretory 209 

pathway localization was challenging to classify. Additional localization details or 210 

observations from alternative independent lines were recorded where relevant ( Table S2 ). 211

We further subgrouped cytosolic and chloroplast proteins ( Fig. 2B-C ). Most cytosolic 228 

proteins were nuclear enriched (17) or homogeneously distributed (13), but we also observed 229 

cytosolic puncta for the putative peroxiredoxin UM012_0167. Chloroplast proteins were 230 

distributed homogeneously (30), non-homogeneously (38) or exclusively to either the 231 

pyrenoid (15) or chloroplast envelope (7; Fig. 2B-C; Table S2 ). Chloroplast proteins were 232 

further classified according to presence, depletion or enrichment in pyrenoid subdomains 233 

(Fig. 2B-C; Table S2 ). We evaluated if Ulva could provide novel or alternative insights into 234 

protein localization compared to Chlamydomonas . We focused on the 151 Ulva genes with a 235 

single ortholog in Chlamydomonas . For the 57 proteins with a localization in both organisms, 236 

21 proteins showed the same spatial pattern, 23 were similar and for 13 the localization was 237 

different ( Table S3 ). The similar category includes proteins with variations in sub-organelle 238 

localization and/or proteins that were localized in two cellular compartments in one organism 239 

but a single compartment in the other. Interestingly, 14 proteins with a similar localization 240 

pattern (61%) had a different sub-pyrenoid enrichment (see below, Table S3 ). 241 

In summary, we used and optimized available molecular tools to enable protein-tagging 242 

screens at scale in a seaweed, which reveals diverse localizations. The enrichment for 243 

chloroplast-localized proteins enabled the discerning of fine sub-chloroplast features. We 244 

observe high expression in many transformed individuals, illustrating that Ulva represents an 245 

excellent model for functional biology in green algae. Broadly, orthologous Ulva and 246 

Chlamydomonas proteins localize to the same or similar locations, further suggesting that 247 

many pCCM components operate at similar spatial scales in chlorophytes. 248

Inorganic carbon delivery to the chloroplast 260 

Ci assimilation from the surrounding environment proceeds via diffusion across the boundary 261 

layer and cell wall to the plasma membrane. As Ulva is mostly found in the intertidal region, 262 

the available Ci species differ between immersion and emersion. When immersed in seawater 263 

under natural conditions (pH 8.1), dissolved HCO 3- is the major Ci molecule. In contrast, 264 

atmospheric CO 2 is more abundant when Ulva is emersed. 74,75 The capacity to continue 265 

photosynthesis and utilize two different Ci sources in two drastically different environments 266

Several CA families, denoted by Greek letters, are pCCM-associated. 78,79 The Ulva genome 268 

encodes twelve CAs: six alpha-type CAs, three beta-type CAs and three gamma-type CAs. 269 

In eukaryotic algae, including Ulva , the gamma-type CAs are mitochondrial and their activity 270 

or role in pCCM is not clearly established; whereas alpha- and beta-type CAs play central 271 

roles at multiple cellular locations. In Chlamydomonas two periplasmic alpha-CAs, CAH1 and 272 

CAH2, catalyze the interconversion of CO 2 with HCO 3-.73,80 Early data using acetazolamide, a 273 

membrane impermeable CA inhibitor, suppressed photosynthesis supporting a similar role 274 

for external CAs in Ulva photosynthesis. 46,81 Five alpha-type CAs are orthologs of 275 

Chlamydomonas CAH1/2, including three paralogs UM108_0014, UM108_0015 and 276 

UM108_0016 that share >98% protein sequence identity. These duplicated genes are lowly 277 

expressed and not identified in whole-cell MS, but we localized the proteins and an additional 278 

alpha-type CA (UM066_0054) to the secretory pathway and plasma membrane ( Fig. 3A ), with 279 

similar localization patterning to secreted proteins in Chlamydomonas .16 The fifth alpha-type 280 

CA, UM028_0029, is expressed and detected in MS data and also predicted to be secreted, 281 

but we did not observe YFP signal in the transformants. Based on these data, Ulva has at 282 

least two and up to five alpha-CAs expressed at the plasma membrane. 283

Inorganic carbon delivery to the pyrenoid and CO 2-recapture 318 

In all characterized pCCMs to date, stromal accumulated HCO 3- is proposed to be shuttled 319 

by BSTs into the lumen of thylakoids that traverse the pyrenoid. Our screen identified two 320 

pyrenoid-associated BSTs. UM004_0038 typically forms two clear puncta at the pyrenoid 321 

periphery, which align with the thylakoid convergent zones we see in our TEMs that 322 

potentially create a large thylakoid surface area for HCO 3- channeling into the PTT ( Fig. 1C-323 

D; Fig. 3D ). UM047_0048 localizes to the PTT itself, forming a clear single band across the 324 

pyrenoid. This is a similar localization to Chlamydomonas CrBST4, which is associated with 325 

maintaining ion homeostasis in the thylakoid lumen, although its substrate is still unclear ( Fig. 326

Structural basis of SUPA1 binding to Rubisco 447 

All Rubisco linker proteins characterized to date have revealed distinct interaction modes 448 

with Rubisco to fulfil their function. 19,104,105 Cryogenic electron microscopy (cryo-EM) resolved 449 

the structure of Ulva Rubisco in complex with a 52-residue fragment corresponding to the 450 

fourth repeat (R4) of SUPA1, centered on the conserved sequence predicted to constitute 451 

the RBM ( Fig. 5E-F ). The reconstruction reached a resolution of 1.8–2.2 Å, with the R4-452 

binding region resolved to ~2.0 Å (Fig. S11 ), enabling unambiguous modeling of the central 453 

18 residues and their interactions with the Rubisco surface ( Fig. 6E-L ). 454 

R4 binds within an extended surface groove formed at the interface of two RbcLs and one 455 

RbcS such that each peptide simultaneously engages three of the 16 subunits of the Rubisco 456 

holoenzyme ( Fig. 6E-G ). Two RbcLs form the structural core of the complex, while one RbcS 457 

contributes to shaping the binding pocket. Within the binding groove, R4 adopts an 458 

elongated conformation interrupted by a short α-helix ( Fig. 6F-H ). There was no evidence of 459 

a helical element in 2D NMR spectra of the isolated R4 fragment ( Fig. S12 ), supporting a 460 

previously undescribed folding-upon-binding mechanism for Rubisco condensation. 461 

The interface combines hydrophobic and electrostatic contacts similar to linker interactions 462 

described in Chlorella and Chlamydomonas . Binding is mediated by two clusters of basic 463

We next quantified the Rubisco–R4 interaction using isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC). 481 

ITC measurements revealed a clear binding signal ( Fig. 6M ) and fitting the integrated heats 482

± 0.15 μM with a stoichiometry of N = 2.8 R4 peptides per Rubisco complex. This 484

sites. Further, the KD is greater than two orders of magnitude higher than cognate single RBM 486

Putative regulators of pyrenoid dynamics 599 

Although the morphology of a pyrenoid looks simple under a light microscope, the complex 600 

dynamics of this structure during cellular processes like cell division was already appreciated 601 

in the first microscopic observations of Ulva .93,94 The rapid (dis)assembly of condensates is 602 

often mediated by (de)phosphorylation of key proteins. Rubisco linkers can be 603 

phosphorylated under low CO 2119 and a kinase is known to disrupt the interaction between 604 

Rubisco and its main linker in Chlamydomonas .120 Interestingly, two chloroplast-localized 605 

proteins, UM011_0186 and UM147_0014, are putative serine/threonine kinases ( Table S1; 606 

Fig. 7C ). UM011_0186 localizes in puncta to the pyrenoid periphery. We also identified a 607 

putative calmodulin-dependent protein kinase (UM114_0035) that localizes to the pyrenoid 608 

periphery in puncta ( Fig. 7C ). Several other protein kinases are present in the Ulva pCCM-609 

associated proteins but were not cloned. However, only one of these (UM018_0146) is 610 

predicted to localize to the chloroplast ( Table S2 ). 611 

Since phosphorylation is a key regulator of pyrenoid dissolution, it is likely that removing 612 

phosphorylation leads to recondensation. In our screen we identified a chloroplast protein 613 

phosphatase 2c (PP2C) family member, UM034_0045, that localizes throughput the 614 

chloroplast but is found in the pyrenoid ( Fig. 7C ). Interestingly, UM034_0045 contains a 615 

disordered region similar to CO 2-sensing PP2Cs that form condensates upon changes in CO 2616 

levels. 121

tubules and reticulated region of the Chlamydomonas pyrenoid. Independently, the frequent 660

this is an important regulatory hub ( Fig. 4C; Fig. 8; Table S2 ). 662

Several chloroplast proteins rely on metal cofactors and anions. Ion homeostasis is crucial 700

# Perspective 715 

We have integrated large-scale protein tagging with biochemistry and structural biology to 716 

provide a comprehensive overview of pCCM carbon fixation in a seaweed. The most 717 

comprehensive mapping to date has been done in the single cell terrestrial/freshwater alga 718 

Chlamydomonas . Our work is of comparable scale but applies it to a marine intertidal 719 

multicellular seaweed, highlighting core similarities and major differences between pCCM 720 

spatial organization, pyrenoid assembly, function and evolution within and outside of the 721 

green lineage (Fig. 9A-B ). Efficient stable transformation remains rare in algae, and 722 

particularly rare in seaweeds, due to transgene silencing. 60,134 Our work shows that Ulva is 723 

amenable to screening large collections of constructs and thereby overcomes a crucial 724 

throughput barrier previously only possible in Chlamydomonas for the eukaryotic algae. The 725 

observed high transgene expression and success rate of functional constructs further 726 

consolidates Ulva as a transformative, functional biology model. 39 

727 

Our elucidation of the CO 2-fixing pathway in Ulva supports the hypothesis that the inorganic 728 

carbon transport components of the CCM (e.g. Ci transporters, channels and CAs) are likely 729 

of a common, ancient origin in the green lineage ( Fig. 9 A-B ). In stark contrast, our 730 

identification and characterization of SUPA1 and RBM containing proteins suggests that 731 

pyrenoid assembly differs significantly, and supports that Rubisco condensation and 732 

subsequently pyrenoid functionalization likely emerged more recently (Fig. 9B-C ). 11,135 

733 

Comparison of SUPA1, with EPYC1 and CsLinker indicates that the high-level architecture 734 

of Rubisco linker proteins within the core-chlorophytes, composing of five or six RBMs 735 

separated by predicted flexible disordered spacer regions, is conserved but that the 736 

properties of the linkers and emergent properties of their pyrenoids differ significantly. 737 

Through our multi-discipline approach, we have revealed that biomolecular condensation of 738 

Ulva Rubisco is mediated via the unique mechanism of SUPA1 binding to the large and small 739 

subunits. Whilst there are eight potential binding sites only four can be simultaneously 740 

occupied due to binding resulting in steric clashes preventing adjacent site binding. This 741 

contrasts to EPYC1 and CsLinker that respectively bind their cognate RbcS and RbcL at 742 

eight binding sites ( Fig. 9C ). 19,105 Another unique feature of the Ulva system is a structural 743 

change of the RBM upon Rubisco binding, with the central RBM region unfolded in solution 744 

but forming a helical conformation upon binding. Potentially compensating for the reduced 745 

number of binding sites is the higher RBM affinity of SUPA1 compared to EPYC1 and 746 

CsLinker, that in turn may impact material properties, such as viscosity as suggested through 747 

slower FRAP recovery rates. Collectively, this supports that Rubisco condensation, the core 748 

defining feature of a pyrenoid 135 , has convergently evolved in the core-chlorophytes through 749 

multivalent linker proteins but the emergent biophysical properties and co-adopted 750 

accessory proteins show significant differences. These differences in material properties and 751 

accessory proteins may explain the large diversity in pyrenoid ultrastructure between 752 

lineages. 753 

Although we uncovered a comprehensive molecular map of pyrenoid-based seaweed carbon 754 

fixation, our dataset relies heavily on ortholog identification based on sequence similarity. For 755 

example, the pyrenoid also contains a large number of proteins without RBMs in 756 

Chlamydomonas. 53 With our library of tagged lines, additional Ulva -specific pCCM 757

unbiased methods like affinity purification and proximity labeling. These methods might shed 759

The green seaweeds (Ulvophyceae) display an interesting diversity in cytology and habitat 762 

preference (marine, brackish and even terrestrial), including lineages without 763 

pyrenoids. 54,136,137 Since pCCMs are the product of convergent evolution that occurred after 764 

the diversification of most lineages, this variability likely translates to pCCM variations. For 765 

example, terrestrial lineages likely rely more on CO 2 diffusion and less on active HCO 3-

766 

transport compared to aquatic organisms, similar to pyrenoid-containing hornworts. 20 The 767 

identification of additional linkers or alternative mechanisms to condensate Rubisco promises 768 

to further reveal principles underlying the convergent evolution of pyrenoids. The green 769 

seaweeds could therefore become important model organisms to better understand the 770 

molecular variation and evolution of carbon fixation pathways. 771 

A large focus of understanding pyrenoid-based CCMs has been with a view to translating 772 

these mechanisms into C 3 crop plants to improve photosynthetic yields. 14,138,139 Our work 773 

provides a powerful insight into how a pCCM is assembled and functions in a multicellular 774 

system. Combined with the recent advances in understanding hornwort pCCM structure and 775 

function 20,57 , these advances are providing a framework for the requirements to functionalize 776 

engineered pyrenoids in plants. 14,139,140 Additionally, the structurally simpler organization of 777 

the Ulva pyrenoid compared to the model template of Chlamydomonas , with a single planar 778 

PTT, two starch plates and inferred reduced structural protein set, and validated multicellular 779 

functionality make an Ulva -based pCCM engineering strategy an exciting prospect. 780 

In summary, seaweeds are keystone organisms that play fundamental roles in marine 781 

ecosystems and global biogeochemical cycles, but until now have been recalcitrant to robust 782 

large-scale genetic manipulation. Removing this limitation in Ulva now allows the detailed 783 

elucidation of biological pathways for the first time in multicellular algae. Beyond a784 

fundamental understanding of the fixation of inorganic carbon into algal biomass, the work 785 

we present here is a starting point to understand why Ulva thrives in coastal ecosystems 786 

worldwide and how it can dominate during blooms. 787
