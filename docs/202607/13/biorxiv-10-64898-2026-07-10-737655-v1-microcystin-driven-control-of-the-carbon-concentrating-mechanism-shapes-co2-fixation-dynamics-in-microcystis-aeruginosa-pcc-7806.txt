Abstract 19 

Bloom-forming cyanobacteria thrive in highly dynamic light environments, yet the 20 

mechanisms enabling rapid acclimation to fluctuating irradiance remain poorly understood. 21 

Here, we compared light acclimation in the bloom-forming cyanobacterium Microcystis 22 

aeruginosa PCC 7806 and the non-bloom-forming model cyanobacterium Synechocystis sp. 23 

PCC 6803 and investigated the role of the cyanobacterial toxin microcystin (MC) and its in 24 

vivo binding partner RubisCO in this process. Whereas Synechocystis grew faster under 25 

sustained high light, Microcystis performed better under low light and responded to transient 26 

high-light exposure with a remarkably rapid increase in photosynthetic activity and glycogen 27 

accumulation. These responses were markedly attenuated in an MC-deficient mutant. 28 

Although RubisCO from Microcystis exhibited pronounced light-dependent changes in 29 

activity, MC had only minor effects on RubisCO catalysis, arguing against a direct role in 30 

regulating enzyme function. Instead, extracellular MC elicited a transient transcriptional 31 

program characterized by induction of inorganic carbon acquisition systems, including the 32 

high-affinity bicarbonate transporter BCT1, consistent with activation of the carbon-33 

concentrating mechanism (CCM) and enhanced carbon fixation in vivo . MC further 34 

stimulated the expression of photosynthesis-related genes, and altered carboxysome 35 

organization, and promoted extracarboxysomal localization of RubisCO. Together, our 36 

findings identify MC as a light-responsive signaling molecule that coordinates CCM activity, 37 

carbon acquisition, and photosynthetic acclimation, thereby enhancing adaptation 38 

of Microcystis to fluctuating irradiance and potentially contributing to its ecological success 39 

in cyanobacterial blooms. 40

Introduction 42 

> 43

Cyanobacteria are integral to freshwater ecosystems, supporting primary production and 44 

nutrient cycling, yet excessive bloom formation leads to a deterioration in water quality, 45 

reduces biodiversity, and impairs fisheries and recreation ( 1). Microcystis is an important 46 

taxon that forms harmful algal blooms (cyanoHABs), with its negative impacts primarily 47 

attributable to the production of microcystins (MCs)—hepatotoxins that threaten drinking 48 

water supplies, and human health ( 2). Microcystis spp. exhibits pronounced genotypic and 49 

phenotypic plasticity, enabling rapid adaptation to changing environmental conditions, 50 

including buoyancy regulation, a key trait for bloom formation ( 3). This flexibility allows it 51 

to optimize access to light and nutrients within the water column and to persist under highly 52 

variable environmental conditions. Genomic plasticity in Microcystis spp. notably includes 53 

variations in bicarbonate transport genes, diversifying its capacity for inorganic carbon 54 

acquisition under changing conditions ( 4). It also encompasses diverse biosynthetic gene 55 

clusters for a specialized metabolism, including those responsible for MC production ( 5). 56 

Single-colony sequencing identified at least 18 distinct genotypes within the 57 

phylum Microcystis , with MC production unevenly distributed among them ( 6). Because 58 

bloom toxicity largely depends on the proportion of toxic genotypes, differences in the 59 

adaptive strategies of toxic and non-toxic Microcystis strains have been used to predict 60 

bloom toxicity under climate change conditions and to inform management strategies ( 7). 61 

Although MC is toxic to potential grazers, its primary role as a defense against eukaryotes is 62 

increasingly questioned, partly because its early evolutionary origin suggests additional 63 

physiological or ecological functions ( 8). 64 

> 65

Genomic variability and the marked differences observed among various Microcystis strains 66 

in their adaptation to different inorganic carbon conditions are particularly noteworthy given 67 

growing evidence linking MC to the regulation of photosynthetic CO 2 fixation. For example, 68 

the MC-producing wild-type strain M. aeruginosa PCC 7806 and the MC-deficient mutant 69 

ΔmcyB differ markedly in their ability to reduce dissolved CO₂ at low concentrations ( 9) and 70 

exhibit transcriptomic, proteomic, and metabolomic differences related to the Calvin– 71 

Benson–Bassham (CBB) cycle and bicarbonate uptake transport ( 10-12 ). These differences 72

deficient mutants displaying a distinct high-light phenotype ( 12 ). While the mechanistic role 74 

of MC under high-light conditions remains unresolved, two MC-related phenomena have 75 

been observed under high-light conditions: increased binding of MC to proteins, including 76

82 

Here, we investigated the role of microcystin and its effects on RubisCO both in vitro and in 83 

vivo , while also incorporating the non-bloom-forming model 84 

cyanobacterium Synechocystis sp. PCC6803 into our analyses. Our findings indicate that MC 85 

does not directly affect RubisCO activity in vitro ; however, extracellular MC appears to 86 

enhance CO 2 fixation in vivo , at least in part through the induction of genes encoding 87 

bicarbonate uptake transporters and by influencing RubisCO localization. Collectively, our 88 

results highlight the complex interplay between MC, the carbon concentrating mechanism 89 

(CCM), and RubisCO, and provide new mechanistic insights into the non-canonical high-90 

light acclimation strategies of the notorious bloom-forming cyanobacterium Microcystis .91

Rapid High-Light Acclimation Distinguishes Microcystis from Synechocystis and Depends on 95 

Microcystin 96 

To assess the effect of MC on low- and high-light acclimation of the bloom-forming 97 

cyanobacterium Microcystis, we monitored growth of the laboratory strain Microcystis 98 

aeruginosa PCC7806 and its MC-free ΔmcyB mutant. As a control we included the non-99 

bloom-forming model cyanobacterium Synechocystis sp. PCC6803 and its fully segregated 100 

rbcLXS replacement mutant variant (RMA) that expresses the RubisCO coding sequence 101 

from Microcystis aeruginosa PCC7806 from a chromosomal integration at the native rbc 102

significantly faster than either Synechocystis strain (Figs. 1B and C). However, MC had no 104

of the MC-deficient Microcystis ΔmcyB mutant was significantly impaired relative to the 110

> 112

In addition to the long-term growth analyses under continuous illumination, we examined the 113 

short-term response of all four genotypes to a shift from low to high light. To this end, we 114 

monitored oxygen evolution as a proxy for photosynthetic activity and glycogen 115 

accumulation as indicator for carbon assimilation (Fig. 1D). Following transfer to high light, 116 

Microcystis very rapidly increased its photosynthetic activity and concomitantly exhibited 117 

fast and strong glycogen accumulation. Both responses were delayed and attenuated in the 118 

MC-deficient mutant. In contrast, Synechocystis did not display a similarly dynamic response 119 

but instead exhibited a more gradual and stable, albeit weaker, acclimation to high light. 120 

Notably, the rapid response of Microcystis was transient. After only one hour of high-light 121 

exposure, both oxygen evolution and glycogen accumulation declined sharply. This decline 122 

was likewise delayed and attenuated in the MC-deficient mutant. In both assays, the RMA 123 

strain showed severe impairment compared to the other strains. This indicates that despite the 124 

high sequence conservation of RubisCO between Microcystis and Synechocystis (97.2% and 125 

84.4% similarity for RbcL and RbcS, respectively), the Microcystis variant cannot serve as a 126 

full functional replacement of the Synechocystis form. Taken together these findings show 127 

that MC plays a role both in long-term and short-term high-light acclimation of the bloom-128 

forming cyanobacterium Microcystis . The exceptionally rapid short-term response to high 129 

light is particularly noteworthy, as Microcystis clearly outperforms Synechocystis during the 130 

initial high light acclimation phase. 131

To determine whether the pronounced differences in light acclimation could be attributed to 134 

intrinsic differences in RubisCO catalysis, we purified the enzyme from all four genotypes 135 

(Fig. 2A) and characterized its biochemical properties. All four RubisCO forms were 136 

recovered in their canonical L 8S8 holoenzyme conformation (Fig. 2B). Moreover, the three 137 

enzymes carrying the Microcystis amino acid sequence exhibited similarly high CO₂/O₂ 138 

specificity factors, among the highest reported for cyanobacterial RubisCOs (Fig. 2C). 139 

Importantly, these specificity factors were independent of whether RubisCO was isolated 140 

from MC-producing cells (wild-type Microcystis ) or from MC-free backgrounds (Δ mcyB and 141 

RMA), indicating that MC does not directly alter the intrinsic catalytic properties of the 142 

enzyme. To test this more directly, we measured RubisCO carboxylase activity by 143 

quantifying 3-phosphoglycerate (3PGA) production in enzyme assays performed in the 144 

presence or absence of purified MC. In parallel, MC-binding to the two Microcystis RubisCO 145 

variants was confirmed by immunoblot analysis (Fig. S2). We found an in vitro inhibiting 146 

effect of MC on wild-type Microcystis RubisCO but only negligible effects on all other 147 

RubisCO preparations (Fig. 2D). As these RubisCOs were isolated from high-light grown 148 

cultures, we next compared 3PGA levels produced by RubisCO isolated from cells grown in 149 

parallel under either low light or high light. Whereas Synechocystis RubisCO showed little 150 

variation in activity between the two light regimes, Microcystis RubisCO displayed 151 

pronounced light-dependent plasticity, particularly in the MC-deficient Δ mcyB mutant. 152 

RubisCO isolated from low-light-grown Δ mcyB cells exhibited substantially higher activity 153 

than the enzyme isolated from cells grown under high light. Notably, RubisCO purified from 154 

the RMA strain did not reproduce this light-dependent activity pattern under either condition. 155 

This observation is particularly striking because the RubisCO enzymes from Δ mcyB and 156 

RMA are identical in amino acid sequence and were both isolated from cells that had never 157 

been exposed to MC. Nevertheless, they exhibited marked differences in carboxylase 158 

activity, especially when purified from low-light-grown cultures. These findings indicate that 159 

factors beyond MC itself contribute to the observed differences in RubisCO activity. 160

end, MC was added to wild-type Microcystis cultures at a concentration of 100 ng mL⁻¹, 163 

corresponding to extracellular levels detected during diurnal experiments ( 14 ). We then 164

Together, these results demonstrate that MC does not directly modulate RubisCO catalysis in 172

Extracellular microcystin coordinates carbon acquisition and photosynthetic responses 177 

during high-light acclimation 178 

To further elucidate the role of MC in the high-light acclimation of Microcystis , we 179 

sequenced the transcriptomes of the wild type and Δ mcyB mutant in the presence or absence 180 

of externally added MC under either low-light conditions or following a shift from low to 181 

high irradiance. To capture the pronounced dynamics observed after the high-light transition 182 

(Figs. 1D and 2F), samples were collected 1 and 3 h after the shift. Under both low- and 183 

high-light conditions, the wild type and Δ mcyB mutant exhibited distinct transcriptional 184 

profiles, as reflected by their clear separation in principal coordinate analyses (Fig. 3A). 185 

Addition of MC had virtually no effect under low-light. In contrast, under high light it 186 

induced extensive transcriptional reprogramming in the Δ mcyB mutant. Notably, MC-treated 187 

mutant samples shifted toward the wild-type high-light transcriptome, suggesting that 188 

extracellular MC can at least partially restore the wild-type transcriptional response to high 189 

light (Fig. 3A). Analysis of MC-responsive genes across COG functional categories 190 

confirmed that the strongest transcriptional effects occurred in the mutant under high-light 191 

exposure, consistent with a broad cellular response to MC. This response was already 192 

markedly attenuated after 3 h, highlighting the transient nature of MC-mediated regulation 193 

(Fig. S3, dataset S1). Among the four most affected categories, Mobilome (X), Defense 194

Genes encoding RubisCO itself were largely unaffected, although rbcS was slightly 200 

downregulated. Most strikingly, extracellular MC strongly affected carbon acquisition and 201 

CCM-related genes. After 1 h of high-light exposure, MC addition induced the major 202 

inorganic carbon uptake systems ( bct1 , bicA , and to a lesser extent ndhI ), carboxysome-203 

associated ccm genes, the periplasmic carbonic anhydrase gene ecaB , and regulatory genes of 204 

the CBB cycle (Fig. 3B). A notable exception was cp12 , which was strongly downregulated. 205 

Because CP12 represses key CBB-cycle enzymes, its downregulation is consistent with 206 

enhanced carbon fixation and activation of the carbon-concentrating machinery ( 16 ). MC 207 

also stimulated the expression of many photosynthesis-related genes in the mutant under 208 

high-light conditions, including the photosystem I reaction center genes psaAB and several 209 

photosystem II components (Figs. 3B and S4). At the same time, several accessory 210 

photosystem I genes, particularly psaC , psaE , and psaK , were repressed. Consistent with the 211 

upregulation of photosynthesis and carbon uptake, genes involved in glycogen biosynthesis 212 

were also induced under high light in mutant cells (Figs. 3B and S4). In the wild type, by 213 

contrast, only a small number of genes responded to externally added MC. These included a 214 

strong upregulation of the high affinity bicarbonate transporter BCT1 and a marked 215 

downregulation of some photosystem 1 genes in response to MC addition after a high light 216 

shift (Fig. 3B). The low affinity, constitutive bicarbonate transporter BicA was also MC-217 

responsive in the wild type, albeit only under low-light conditions, where its expression 218 

increased. (Fig. 3B). The generally weaker effect of MC on the wild-type strain likely 219 

reflects its native exposure to external MC rendering it less responsive to additional MC (Fig. 220 

3A). 221

Comparison of the high-light response in Microcystis in the presence and absence of MC 235 

suggests that several hallmark acclimation processes ( 18 ) are accelerated or amplified by 236 

MC. These include earlier induction of the bicarbonate uptake systems BCT1 and BicA, 237 

enhanced expression of photosystem II genes such as psbA , and modulation of the 238 

photosystem I/photosystem II balance through differential regulation of PSI components 239 

(Fig. S4). Together, these findings suggest that extracellular MC triggers a rapid but transient 240 

stimulation of photosynthesis and carbon acquisition. Although this response may reduce the 241 

extent of PSI downregulation that normally limits photodamage under high light, repression 242 

of specific PSI subunits such as PsaC and PsaE may still contribute to protection against 243 

excess light stress. Several aspects of the MC response also differ from canonical high-light 244 

acclimation responses described in other cyanobacteria. Most notably, high-light-induced 245 

expression of cp12 —previously reported in S. elongatus PCC 7942 and likewise observed in 246 

the Microcystis strains examined here—was suppressed by MC ( 19 ). In Microcystis ,247 

however, this response may be functionally compensated by the concurrent upregulation of a 248 

CBS-CP12-encoding gene, whose product appears to be at least partially redundant with 249 

CP12 in regulating the CBB cycle. Overall, the transcriptomic data support a model in which 250 

extracellular MC acts as a transient signal that promotes carbon acquisition, photosynthetic 251 

activity, and high-light acclimation, thereby facilitating the rapid adaptation of Microcystis to 252 

fluctuating light conditions. 253

To determine whether externally added MC influences key CCM components beyond gene 256 

expression, we analyzed the localization of RbcL (the large subunit of RubisCO, Fig. 4A-C) 257 

and CcmK (the major structural component of the carboxysome, Fig. 4D-F) by 258 

immunofluorescence microscopy. Specifically, we asked whether exogenous MC could 259 

restore the differences in RubisCO localization previously observed between the wild type 260 

and the MC-deficient mutant ( 15 ). RubisCO localization was highly plastic, particularly in 261 

the MC-producing wild type. We classified the observed patterns into four localization states: 262 

(i) carboxysomal, (ii) diffuse extracarboxysomal, (iii) mixed 263 

carboxysomal/extracarboxysomal, and (iv) peripheral (Fig. 4A), with peripheral localization 264 

typically occurring together with diffuse or mixed patterns. The MC-deficient mutant showed 265 

predominantly carboxysomal RubisCO under both light conditions (Fig. 4B). In contrast, the 266 

wild type displayed substantially more mixed carboxysomal/extracarboxysomal and 267 

peripheral localization (Fig. 4A). While the overall localization pattern in the wild type was 268 

similar under low and high light, the extent of peripheral RubisCO localization increased 269 

under high light (Fig. 4A). Consistent with the increased extracarboxysomal RubisCO pool, 270 

carboxysome shell genes were downregulated in the wild-type strain under high-light 271 

conditions (Fig. S4). The addition of MC had only modest effects on RubisCO localization in 272 

the wild type, causing a gradual reduction in peripheral localization. In contrast, MC 273 

treatment elicited a distinct response in the DmcyB mutant under high light. Alongside 274 

carboxysomal localization, most cells developed pronounced a diffuse extracarboxysomal 275 

RubisCO localization, partially restoring the wild-type phenotype. Notably, however, 276 

peripheral RubisCO localization was not reconstituted. 277 

> 278

A similar pattern was observed for carboxysome organization. In the wild type, the 279 

carboxysome shell protein CcmK frequently dispersed from discrete foci into more diffuse 280 

cytoplasmic regions, irrespective of light conditions (Fig. 4D). By contrast, carboxysome 281 

integrity was generally maintained in the MC-deficient mutant, with CcmK dispersal 282 

becoming apparent only after MC addition under high-light conditions (Fig. 4D). Together, 283 

these observations suggest that MC promotes a more dynamic organization of both RubisCO 284 

and carboxysome components, especially under high-light conditions. 285

Discussion 287 

288 

In this study, we identify microcystin (MC) as a key contributor to the rapid acclimation 289 

of Microcystis aeruginosa PCC7806 to fluctuating irradiance. Compared 290 

with Synechocystis , Microcystis displayed a pronounced but transient response to high-light 291 

shifts, and this dynamic acclimation was strongly attenuated in the MC-deficient mutant. 292 

Because rapid adjustment to fluctuating light is thought to promote bloom formation and 293 

buoyancy regulation ( 3), MC likely contributes to the ecological success of Microcystis .294 

295 

Although previous work already demonstrated enhanced high-light tolerance of the MC-296 

producing wild type relative to the MC-deficient mutant ( 12 ), the underlying mechanism 297 

remained unresolved. In the present study, we therefore tested two hypotheses which are not 298 

necessarily mutually exclusive: first, that MC binding to RubisCO contributes 299 

mechanistically to enhanced high-light adaptation, and second, that extracellular MC 300 

functions as a signaling molecule governing the acclimation response. Although RubisCO 301 

activity showed pronounced light-dependent plasticity in Microcystis , our biochemical 302 

analyses provide little evidence for direct MC-mediated regulation. Instead, the lack of 303 

comparable regulation in the RMA strain suggests that other Microcystis -specific cellular 304 

factors modulate RubisCO activity, whereas MC likely acts upstream of carbon fixation 305 

rather than by directly altering RubisCO catalysis. 306 

307 

Consistent with a signaling role, extracellular MC induced a transient increase in 3PGA 308 

accumulation under high-light conditions in vivo and elicited a pronounced transcriptional 309 

response, particularly in the MC-deficient mutant. Transcriptomic analyses indicate that 310 

extracellular MC primarily targets the carbon acquisition machinery rather than RubisCO 311 

itself, thereby transiently enhancing inorganic carbon supply during high-light exposure. This 312 

response was substantially stronger than that observed in our previous study, where MC-313 

dependent transcriptional effects were limited to a cryptic polyketide synthase biosynthetic 314 

gene cluster (BGC) following 24 h of low-light incubation ( 10 ). Together, these results 315

> 318

Importantly, MC-induced regulation of carbon acquisition occurred as part of a broader 319 

transcriptional reprogramming. Although MC has frequently been linked to stress adaptation 320 

(20 ), it had limited effects on canonical oxidative stress markers. Instead, MC transiently and 321 

reciprocally regulated thioredoxin-related proteins, including a thioredoxin A variant and its 322 

associated CBS-CP12 protein implicated in CBB cycle regulation ( 17 ), while repressing the 323 

canonical cp12 gene. These coordinated but opposing changes in functionally related proteins 324 

suggest a metabolic transition that may optimize CO 2 fixation during high-light 325 

exposure. MC-dependent regulation also extended to photosynthetic gene expression, 326 

suggesting coordinated remodeling of carbon fixation and the photosynthetic apparatus. The 327 

transient nature of both the transcriptional response and photosynthetic stimulation supports 328 

the view that MC initiates a rapid acclimation program rather than establishing a stable 329 

physiological state (Fig. 5). 330 

> 331

Exogenous MC also partially restored the characteristic RubisCO and carboxysome 332 

organization of the wild type ( 15 ), demonstrating that extracellular MC regulates CCM 333 

architecture in addition to CCM gene expression. However, cytoplasmic membrane-334 

associated RubisCO was not recovered, distinguishing extracellular signaling effects from 335 

intracellular MC-dependent processes (Fig. 5). Because this peripheral RubisCO state is 336 

closely associated with MC binding ( 14 ) it likely represents a distinct intracellular function 337 

of MC. Although the underlying mechanisms remain unclear, membrane-associated 338 

extracarboxysomal RubisCO in the wild type coincided with highly efficient glycogen 339 

accumulation. We therefore propose that this configuration, together with enhanced 340 

bicarbonate uptake, constitutes an alternative and potentially more flexible CCM that enables 341 

rapid responses to fluctuating environmental conditions. Such a system may contribute to the 342 

ecological success of Microcystis by facilitating transitions between periods of intense 343 

photosynthetic activity and subsequent glycogen utilization, processes that are crucial for 344 

buoyancy regulation of the colony-forming cyanobacteria. The signaling cascade underlying 345 

MC perception and extracellular release remains unknown, and future studies will be 346

Materials and methods 358 

359 

Cultivation of cyanobacteria 360 

Cyanobacterial liquid cultures were maintained in Erlenmeyer flasks in BG 11 medium ( 21 )361 

at 25°C, under constant white light illumination of 10 μE/m 2s (i.e. “low light conditions”) at 362 

ambient CO 2. The mutant strains Microcystis aeruginosa PCC7806 ∆mcyB and Synechocystis 363 

sp. PCC6803 RMA were cultivated with chloramphenicol at a final concentration of 5 μg/μl. 364 

Axenicity of cyanobacterial cultures was regularly assessed by streaking aliquots on solid 365 

R2A agar medium and subsequent 24h incubation at 30°C, 37°C and 22°C. For high light 366 

experiments, cultures were grown at constant white light illumination of 250 μE/m 2s unless 367 

stated otherwise. Growth curves were recorded from liquid cultures grown in biological 368 

triplicates in 100 mL Erlenmeyer flasks under different light conditions. Samples of 1 mL 369 

were taken under sterile conditions and optical density at 750 nm (OD 750 ) was measured 370
