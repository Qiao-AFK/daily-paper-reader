## Abstract 23 

Rubisco catalyzes the primary CO 2-fixing reaction of the biosphere, yet its competing 24 

oxygenation reaction reduces net global carbon fixation and has resisted direct 25 

exploration in living cells. Here, we engineer an auxotrophic Escherichia coli strain in 26 

which 2-phosphoglycolate, the direct product of Rubisco oxygenation, becomes 27 

essential for growth, making bacterial fitness a quantitative proxy for oxygenation flux 28 

in vivo . This provides direct access to catalytic selectivity, something previously 29 

inaccessible to carboxylation-coupled assays. The platform enables screening of 30 

phylogenetically diverse Form II Rubisco and phosphoribulokinase (Prk) variants 31 

circumventing protein purification and extensive in vitro characterization. Adaptive 32 

laboratory evolution under oxygenation-selective pressure identified two mutations: 33 

Rubisco M115I genetically rebalances the in vivo carboxylation/oxygenation trade-off 34 

(resulting in 6-fold reduction in k cat, C), while Prk N216T improves overall flux without 35 

altering selectivity. This platform makes Rubisco's least-studied catalytic function 36 

selectable and evolvable in vivo , opening the carboxylation/oxygenation trade-off to 37 

systematic genetic dissection and engineering. 38

## Introduction 40 

Enzyme promiscuity is widely present in nature and challenges the textbook view of 41 

enzymes as exceptionally specific catalysts (1, 2). The most abundant enzyme in the 42 

biosphere, ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate (RuBP) carboxylase/oxygenase (Rubisco), with 43 

a total mass of approximately 0.7 Gt and responsible for more than 99% of the global 44 

CO 2 fixation (3, 4), is no exception. Alongside its essential carboxylation reaction, 45 

Rubisco catalyzes a competing oxygenation that directly limits the efficiency of carbon 46 

fixation on a global scale. 47 

Rubisco emerged in a CO 2-rich atmosphere prior to the Great Oxidation Event. With 48 

the rise of oxygenic photosynthesis, it was increasingly challenging to discriminate 49 

between CO 2 and O 2 as atmospheric CO 2 declined and O 2 levels rose (5). Shaped by 50 

diverse selective pressures across environmental niches (6), Rubisco has undergone 51 

gradual albeit extremely slow improvements in carboxylation efficiency (7). These 52 

include for example, enhancements in catalytic kinetics in plants (7); structural 53 

modifications such as the recruitment of an additional subunit in Form I Rubisco to 54 

improve CO 2/O 2 discrimination (8); and the adoption of carbon-concentrating 55 

mechanisms in diverse organisms to boost local CO 2 availability (6, 9). 56 

Despite these evolutionary adaptations, Rubisco retains its oxygenase activity, with 57 

oxygenation events exceeding 20% of total catalytic turnovers (5, 10, 11), that 58 

significantly reduce net CO 2 fixation. Oxygenation adds O 2 to RuBP, producing one 59 

molecule of 3-phosphoglycerate (3PG) and one molecule of 2-phosphoglycolate 60 

(2PG). The latter is highly reactive and recycled via photorespiration, a process that 61 

consumes ATP and reducing power while releasing previously fixed CO 2, thereby 62 

reducing the overall efficiency of carbon fixation (10, 12, 13). 63 

To mitigate the impact of Rubisco oxygenation, synthetic biology efforts have targeted 64 

either the enzyme itself through, for example, directed evolution (14-17), or the 65 

photorespiration process by introducing bypass routes in metabolism that prevent CO 266 

loss (13, 18-21). Yet, oxygenation remains virtually unavoidable. Rubisco’s kinetic 67 

parameters appear constrained by trade-offs among turnover, substrate affinity, and 68 

specificity (4, 22-26), and while its oxygenation mechanism is understood (23, 27, 28), 69 

studying it has historically relied on laborious in vitro approaches that strip the enzyme 70 

of its cellular context. 71 

Recently, growth-coupled in vivo microbial platforms have begun addressing this 72 

limitation for carboxylation, enabling sequence-fitness mapping at scale (17). Growth-73 

coupling offers a route to study enzymes in their physiological context: by tailoring 74 

microbial fitness to depend on a specific enzymatic reaction, growth rate becomes a 75 

living proxy for catalytic activity in vivo (29-31). The same principle extends to 76 

continuous selective pressure, enabling directed laboratory evolution under defined 77 

metabolic constraints (29, 30, 32-36). For Rubisco specifically, the established ∆ rpiAB 78 

(∆rpi ) platform exploits this logic by coupling ribulose 5-phosphate (Ru5P) 79 

detoxification to Prk–Rubisco activity (17, 37), but cannot resolve the carboxylation– 80

oxygenation partition that underlies Rubisco's central catalytic trade-off, because both 81 

reactions consume RuBP. Therefore, oxygenase activity has remained an unresolved 82 

intrinsic feature rather than a tractable signal. Its physiological consequences and 83 

evolutionary potential in vivo have therefore not been systematically explored. 84 

Here we developed a growth-coupled platform coupling bacterial fitness directly to 85 

Rubisco oxygenation. We repurposed an E. coli auxotrophic metabolic sensor 86 

originally designed for glycolate sensing (38) to harbor heterologous prk and Rubisco 87 

genes. In this context, Rubisco oxygenation synthesizes 2PG and, through 88 

downstream reactions, replenishes the glycolate pool in the auxotroph. This provides 89 

direct access to catalytic selectivity (previously impossible with traditional Rubisco-90 

coupled assays) enabling systematic dissection of sequence determinants controlling 91 

oxygenase activity across Rubisco phylogenies. Adaptive laboratory evolution (ALE) 92 

identified two mutations with complementary functions: one (located in Rubisco) 93 

rebalanced the in vivo carboxylation/oxygenation selectivity, while the other (in Prk) 94 

enhanced flux irrespective of the selectivity. Altogether, this work establishes a 95 

tractable in vivo framework for dissecting and modifying Rubisco’s least-studied 96 

catalytic function. 97

## Results 98 

In silico demonstration of growth complementation through Rubisco 99 

oxygenation 100 

Building on a compact E. coli metabolic model (39), we previously designed mutant 101 

strains in which glyoxylate serves as a key metabolic intermediate for engineering 102 

glycolate auxotrophies (38). Here, we employ the same metabolic reconstruction to 103 

assess if Rubisco oxygenation can complement growth in silico in the absence of 104 

externally added glycolate. 105 

We selected the auxotrophy design with the highest sensitivity to glycolate i.e., the 106 

lowest glycolate flux required to restore growth, corresponding to the combined 107 

deletions of isocitrate lyase ( ∆ICL ) and serine hydroxymethyltransferase ( ∆GHMT2r )108 

(Fig. 1A ). To this background, which underlies the previously described strains 109 

C1+GLY-AUX and GLY-AUX (38), we added BiGG reactions for Prk and for the two 110 

Rubisco half-reactions, carboxylation (RBC, C) and oxygenation (RBC, O). To probe the 111 

contribution of each reaction independently of total enzyme activity, we constrained 112 

the model so that total Rubisco flux remained constant while the ratio between the two 113 

activities was scanned across nine orders of magnitude as 10 φ = v(RBC, O) / v(RBC, C), 114 

with  φ ∈ [–4, +4]. 115 

Under this constraint, the predicted growth rate of the GLY-AUX design rose 116 

monotonically with  φ, tracking the increasing oxygenation share ( Fig. 1B ). Because 117 

the strain is auxotrophic for 2PG-derived metabolites and not for 3PG, its growth is 118 

intrinsically insensitive to carboxylation whereas oxygenation sets the predicted 119 

growth rate. The model thus supports stoichiometrically that, in this genetic 120 

background, growth depends specifically on Rubisco's oxygenation flux ( Extended 121 

Data Fig. 1A ). Using literature values of Rubisco's SC/O and the expected intracellular 122 

CO 2/O 2 ratio under ambient conditions ( Supplementary File 1 ), we estimated a 123 

physiologically realistic  φ range of − 0.8 to +0.4, corresponding to roughly ≈10 to ≈20% 124 

of total Rubisco flux going through oxygenation, substantiating that physiologically 125 

relevant oxygenation rates are sufficient to complement growth. 126 

To test whether this selectivity is a property of the strain rather than of the Rubisco 127 

reactions themselves, we examined the predicted growth response of the established 128 

∆rpiAB (∆RPI ) sensor background as a function of independently varied carboxylation 129 

and oxygenation fluxes ( Supplementary Fig. S1A, C ). In ∆ RPI , which uses Rubisco 130 

as a detoxification route for Ru5P (17, 37), predicted growth was positive across the 131 

entire phase plane wherever either flux was nonzero: carboxylation and oxygenation 132 

contributed equivalently because both reactions consume Ru5P and resolve the 133 

metabolic dead-end regardless of product identity. In contrast, the GLY-AUX design 134 

displayed a clear dependence on the oxygenase reaction for growth, with insensitivity 135 

to the carboxylation reaction ( Supplementary Fig. S1B, D ). Having established 136 

stoichiometrically that growth in the GLY-AUX background can be supported by 137 

Rubisco oxygenation alone, we next sought to study this coupling in vivo .138

A glycolate auxotroph couples growth to Rubisco oxygenation 139 

In vivo Rubisco screening in E. coli requires co-expression with prk , since the Rubisco 140 

substrate RuBP is not naturally present in E. coli ’s metabolic network (40). Because 141 

RuBP accumulation is detrimental, Rubisco’s activity alleviates this toxicity and 142 

thereby enables viable growth (41). However, when the system does not strictly 143 

depend on the Prk–Rubisco module, prk often acquires deleterious mutations (42). 144 

Previous approaches to probe Rubisco’s activity in vivo , including pentose utilization 145 

deficiency ( ∆rpi ) (17, 37) and 3PG auxotrophy (43), cannot distinguish between 146 

carboxylation and oxygenation ( Extended Data Fig. 1B-E ). In contrast, coupling 147 

growth to 2PG metabolism provides a direct and exclusive readout of oxygenase 148 

activity ( Fig. 1A,B Supplementary Fig. S1 ). 2PG is converted to glycolate by the 149 

constitutively expressed phosphoglycolate phosphatase gene ( gph , EC 3.1.3.18) (44), 150 

and we recently developed E. coli glycolate auxotrophs capable of sensing this 151 

metabolite (38), providing a platform for linking Rubisco oxygenation to microbial 152 

fitness. 153 

We therefore transformed the GLY-AUX2 (a derivative of the GLY-AUX strain (38) 154 

lacking the glycine cleavage system ∆ gcvTHP ; Supplementary Fig. S2 ), with the 155 

previously described pCBB plasmid carrying a constitutive prk -Rubisco module ( prk 156 

from Synechococcus elongatus and cbbM from R. rubrum ) (45) ( Fig. 1C ). Deletion of 157 

gcvTHP was motivated by the labeling profile previously observed, which suggested 158 

partial contribution of formate to glycine biosynthesis through reverse activity of the 159 

glycine cleavage system as consequence of heterologous expression of a formate 160 

tetrahydrofolate ligase (38, 46). As shown in Fig. 1D , strain GLY-AUX2 grew only when 161 

the pCBB plasmid was provided and the headspace contained ambient CO 2 (0.04%). 162 

In contrast, growth was impaired at 10% CO 2 (Fig. 1D ). This suggests that at ambient 163 

CO 2, Rubisco catalyzes oxygenation, generating 2PG that is further converted into 164 

glycolate, whereas at 10% CO 2 the enzyme is saturated with CO 2, allowing only 165 

carboxylation and thus preventing growth, thereby matching the in silico predictions. 166 

To verify activity of the Prk-Rubisco module, we transformed pCBB into a ∆ rpi strain 167 

and observed growth only in the presence of the plasmid ( Extended Data Fig. 1B-E ). 168 

Higher growth rates at 10% CO 2 compared to ambient conditions likely reflect both the 169 

higher turnover rate of carboxylation versus oxygenation (47) and the additional 170 

metabolic cost of 2PG recycling when oxygenation occurs. Having confirmed that our 171 

platform selectively senses oxygenation through the activities encoded in the pCBB 172 

plasmid, we next developed a standardized expression platform to streamline cloning 173 

of diverse Prk and Rubisco variants. 174

A diverse set of Prk variants is functionally interchangeable in pCBB2 204 

To assess whether Prk identity limits oxygenation-dependent growth, we cloned eight 205 

variants spanning Alpha-, Beta-, and Gamma-proteobacteria, including both wild-type 206 

and codon-optimized sequences, into plasmid pCBB2 rbc4 (prk1 –prk8 ,207

Growth rates were comparable across all constructs, with no variant significantly 212 

outperforming Prk1 in GLY-AUX2 ( Fig. 2C, Supplementary Fig. S6 ), except for a 213 

marginal difference between prk2 and prk4 (one-way ANOVA; adjusted p = 0.031). 214

Prk sequence and codon optimization, validating pCBB2 as a robust expression 216

Phylogenetically diverse Form II Rubiscos support oxygenation-dependent 218 

growth 219 

Next, we replaced the Rubisco gene from R. rubrum (rbc4 ) in pCBB2 with nine 220 

additional Form II Rubisco variants selected for high kcat values in vitro (51) and 221 

spanning Alpha-, Beta-, Gamma-, and Zeta-proteobacteria (Fig. 2D, E, 222 

Supplementary Fig. S7, Supplementary Data 1 ). Form II Rubiscos were prioritized 223 

for their compatibility with native E. coli chaperones (52) and their relatively high 224 

oxygenation-to-carboxylation ratio compared to other Rubisco forms (53). 225 

We first checked whether the cloned Prk–Rubisco modules were catalytically 226 

functional by assessing growth complementation by the same pCBB2 variants 227 

transformed in the ∆ rpi background, selecting for carboxylation at 10% CO 2. Eight out 228 

of ten variants reached stationary phase within 150 h ( Supplementary Fig. S8 ), 229 

confirming that most modules actively support metabolic flux through the carboxylation 230 

reaction. 231 

We then assessed growth complementation of GLY-AUX2 transformed with each 232 

pCBB2 variant under ambient pCO 2 (0.0004 atm or 0.04% CO 2) in plate-reader 233 

cultivations. Despite confirmed module functionality, only rbc4 and rbc6 from 234 

Sulfurivirga caldicuralii supported growth ( Supplementary Fig. S9 ). We hypothesized 235 

that ineffective flux through Prk–Rubisco, possibly caused by imbalanced protein 236 

stoichiometry, reduced oxygenation activity below the threshold required for growth. 237 

Unlike ∆ rpi , GLY-AUX2 growth depends strictly on oxygenation-derived products with 238 

no alternative metabolic route, and carboxylation and oxygenation compete under 239 

ambient CO 2, together imposing a higher demand on Rubisco oxygenation flux. 240 

Consistent with this interpretation, analysis of predicted TIR values revealed that when 241 

rbc4 was held constant and prk variants were substituted (Fig. 2A-C ), Rubisco-to-Prk 242 

TIR ratios remained within 1–10 arbitrary units across nearly all constructs 243 

(Supplementary Table S2 ). By contrast, fixing prk1 while varying the Rubisco 244 

introduced substantially greater variability, with ratios spanning from 2 to over 200 245 

(Supplementary Table S3 ). Such stoichiometric imbalance could thereby limit 246 

effective oxygenation flux in strain GLY-AUX2, where the metabolic demand on 247 

Rubisco-derived products is high. 248 

We therefore reasoned that oxygen availability in the cultures could compound this 249 

limitation and performed growth complementation experiments in sealed baffled shake 250 

flasks, where evaporation is prevented and any OD 600 increase above background 251 

strictly requires sustained Rubisco-mediated oxygenation. Accordingly, we observed 252 

significant turbidity increases for nearly all tested variants, with rbc4 and rbc6 253 

mediating the highest final OD 600 values ( Fig. 2F ), consistent with plate-reader results. 254 

Growth was modest when the selection strain was transformed with most variants 255 

(OD 600 ≤ 0. 4), except for rbc4 and rbc6 (OD 600 > 1.0). Yet, the data demonstrates that 256 

oxygenation-dependent complementation is achievable across phylogenetically 257 

diverse Form II Rubiscos although the overall performance ceiling of the GLY-AUX2 258

Adaptive laboratory evolution localizes the oxygenation bottleneck to the Prk– 261 

Rubisco module 262 

The modest oxygenation-dependent growth rates achieved with pCBB2 rbc4prk1 in 263 

GLY-AUX2 represented an opportunity to apply the selective pressure of continuous 264 

cultivation to improve module performance in vivo . We therefore subjected GLY-AUX2 265 

harboring pCBB2rbc4prk1 to ALE in turbidostatic mode operated in four independent 266 

reactors (R1–R4) for five weeks ( Fig. 3A, B, Extended Data Fig. 2A ). A consistent 267 

decrease in time between dilutions confirmed progressive improvement in population 268 

growth rate ( Extended Data Fig. 2B, C ). Two parallel turbidostat runs under relaxed 269 

conditions (R5–R6, strain GLY-AUX2 carrying the empty pSEVA221 vector) served as 270 

controls to filter mutations arising from genetic drift or platform adaptation. 271 

Whole-genome sequencing of evolved populations revealed no chromosomal 272 

mutations that could be traced to glycolate or glyoxylate metabolism ( Fig. 3C, 273 

Supplementary Data 2 ), localizing the adaptive bottleneck to the plasmid-borne Prk– 274 

Rubisco module. Plasmid sequencing identified two recurrently selected mutations: 275 

M115I in rbc4 (hereafter rbc4 *) and N216T in prk1 (hereafter prk1 *) ( Fig. 3C, Table 276 

S4 ). In R2, prk1* arose first with rbc4* emerging only after an additional four weeks, 277 

suggesting sequential rather than simultaneous selection ( Table S5 ). 278 

To completely rule out the effect of the chromosomal mutations, we isolated single 279 

GLY-AUX2 evo clones from R1-R4 were cured of the evolved plasmid and retransformed 280 

with the naïve (non-evolved) pCBB2 rbc4prk1 plasmid ( Fig. 3D ). Growth rates were 281 

largely comparable to the parental strain across isolates, with the exception of the R4-282 

derived clone, which showed a modest but statistically significant improvement 283 

(Tukey's multiple comparisons test, adjusted p = 0.036; Fig. 3E, Supplementary Fig. 284 

S10 ). Proteomic analysis of these isolates revealed a consistent upregulation of 285 

proteins involved in glyoxylate and dicarboxylate metabolism in all four clones ( Fig. 286 

3F, G; Supplementary Files 2 and 3 ). Taken together, these results indicate that 287 

chromosomal adaptations (most notably upregulation of glyoxylate metabolism) 288 

contribute marginally to the evolved phenotype in at least one isolate but are unlikely 289 

to account for the substantial fitness gains observed during ALE. We therefore focused 290 

on the plasmid-borne mutations prk1 * and rbc4 * as the primary drivers of improved 291 

oxygenation-dependent growth. 292

Collectively, these results demonstrate that the nonsynonymous N216T substitution in 321

under both selection conditions, Prk1* is most parsimoniously interpreted as relieving 325 

the RuBP-supply bottleneck rather than altering Rubisco selectivity; a reaction-326 

agnostic, flux-level improvement of the Prk–Rubisco module. This inference is 327 

supported by the orthogonal readout from the two selection strains, which jointly 328 

distinguish module-level flux effects from Rubisco-selectivity effects. To gain insight 329 

into the mechanistic basis of the ALE-derived mutation, we examined the structural 330 

context of the substituted residue in Prk. Mapping the mutation onto the Prk1 331 

AlphaFold-structure revealed that N216 is located on the solvent-exposed surface of 332 

the protomer and lies approximately 20–21 Å from the RuBP- and ATP-binding sites, 333 

suggesting that its effects are unlikely to arise from direct alterations of the catalytic 334 

center. Given that Prk1 has been reported to assemble as an octamer (54, 55), we 335 

generated an AlphaFold-based octameric model to assess potential intersubunit 336 

interactions ( Fig. 4G ). In this model, N216 resides at a subunit interface and is 337 

predicted to form a hydrogen bond with S200 from a neighboring protomer. 338

Rbc4 M115I shifts the in vivo balance toward oxygenation 348 

Next, we introduced the M115I substitution in rbc4 within the pCBB2 rbc4prk1* plasmid 349 

and evaluated its impact on oxygenation-dependent growth in the GLY-AUX2 system 350 

(Fig. 5A-C ). At 0.04% CO 2, the plasmid harboring Rbc4* supported only a modest 351

## Discussion 409 

Rubisco's oxygenase activity has been characterized biochemically for decades but 410 

has never been accessed as a direct selection trigger in vivo . Two previously reported 411 

E. coli platforms assay Rubisco activity by coupling RuBP turnover to growth — the 412 

∆gapA strain through a Prk–Rubisco shunt around a glycolytic deficiency (42, 47, 56-413 

58), and ∆ rpi by detoxifying Ru5P accumulation through a plasmid-borne Prk–Rubisco 414 

module (17, 37, 42, 47) — but neither resolves the carboxylation–oxygenation partition 415 

that defines Rubisco's central catalytic trade-off, because growth depends on RuBP 416 

consumption regardless of whether the products are 3PG or 2PG. Here, we developed 417 

a complementary platform based on glycolate auxotrophy. Strain GLY-AUX2 couples 418 

2PG formation to glycine biosynthesis through an engineered glycolate auxotrophy 419 

(38), with deletion of gcvTHP blocking the reverse, CO 2-fixing direction of the glycine 420 

cleavage system that would otherwise supply glycine independently of Rubisco 421 

oxygenation at elevated pCO 2. To our knowledge, no previous strain has coupled 422 

microbial growth specifically to Rubisco oxygenation, enabling both screening and 423 

continuous selection for an activity that has been largely inaccessible to forward-424 

genetic approaches. 425 

Pairing the modification in strain GLY-AUX2 with the ∆rpi deletion allowed us to read 426 

out oxygenation and carboxylation fluxes, respectively, from the same Rubisco variant 427 

in two complementary genetic backgrounds, and this dual readout proved diagnostic 428 

for the ALE-derived substitutions Rbc4* and Prk1*. Rbc4* showed a reduction in 429 

growth for ∆rpi , corroborated by a 6-fold reduction in kcat,C in vitro yet enhanced 430 

oxygenation-dependent growth in GLY-AUX2; this is direct in vivo evidence that a 431 

single amino acid substitution can shift the carboxylation/oxygenation balance of a 432 

Form II Rubisco. M115 lies adjacent to D117, a residue previously implicated in 433 

modulating SC/O through reductions in kcat,C and kcat,C /K C. Structural modelling provides 434 

a plausible mechanistic basis for this phenotype. The M115I substitution alters a 435 

network of hydrophobic contacts involving H44 and E48, both residues proximal to the 436 

active site of R. rubrum Rubisco, in a way that repositions first E48 and consequently 437 

K168 (already forming a hydrogen bond with the RuBP substrate), likely modifying the 438 

native geometry of the catalytic pocket. This rearrangement is consistent with a 439 

reduction in RuBP accommodation without proportional loss of oxygenase activity, 440 

providing a structural rationale for the observed reduction in kcat,C . In the absence of 441 

direct SC/O measurements and an experimentally determined structure of the variant 442 

in complex with the substrates, this interpretation remains tentative. Yet, the in vivo 443 

and in vitro phenotypes establish the carboxylation–oxygenation trade-off 444 

independently of the specific mechanism of action of the ALE-derived mutation. Prk1*, 445 

in contrast, increased growth under both carboxylation- and oxygenation-selective 446 

conditions and is most parsimoniously explained as a non-selectivity-altering 447 

improvement in RuBP supply, which becomes available for Rubisco. This illustrates 448 

that the dual readout also distinguishes selectivity-shifting mutations from generic 449 

fitness improvements. 450

Four features of the platform deserve to be framed as design insights rather than 451 

incidental limitations. First, oxygenation-dependent growth in GLY-AUX2 required 452 

baffled shake flasks to be detectable across the full variant panel; in standard plate-453 

reader cultivation, only two of ten Rubiscos supported measurable growth, while 454 

carboxylation screening in the ∆rpi strain at 10% CO 2 was robust (8/10 variants). This 455 

O 2-sensitivity is mechanistically expected: the assay is selective for oxygenation 456 

precisely because growth tracks RuBP to 2PG flux; therefore, dissolved O 2 is a tunable 457 

selection variable for future high-throughput setups. Second, growth fitness in GLY-458 

AUX2 integrates Rubisco activity with RuBP supply, intracellular CO 2/O 2 partitioning, 459 

enzyme expression and folding, and downstream glyoxylate metabolism. Several 460 

built-in controls anchor the interpretation of this system-level readout: the strain does 461 

not grow without the Prk–Rubisco module ( Extended Data Fig. 1 F-G ), (unless 462 

evolved) does not grow at elevated CO 2 where Rubisco oxygenation is suppressed 463 

(Fig. 1D ), and is stoichiometrically predicted by modeling to depend exclusively on the 464 

oxygenation flux ( Fig. 1A, B, Extended Data Fig. 1A, Supplementary Fig. S1 ). 465 

Attribution of fitness differences across variants is further anchored by the orthogonal 466 

∆rpi readout, which distinguishes selectivity-altering from generic flux-altering 467 

mutations — as illustrated by the Rbc4* and Prk1* variants, respectively — and by in 468 

vitro characterization of selected variants, as for the kcat, C measurement on Rbc1*. 469 

Third, the pCBB2 expression architecture was tuned against a single Prk–Rubisco pair 470 

(rbc4 and prk1), and the resulting RBS strengths are unlikely to be optimal across the 471 

full range of screened variants; reaching plate-reader-detectable oxygenation growth 472 

for each variant will likely require a per-variant round of RBS retuning, which the 473 

modular design of pCBB2 supports. Fourth, the two sensor strains impose different 474 

selective demands on the heterologous module. In ∆ rpi , carboxylation needs only 475 

supplying a small fraction of total carbon flux in order to complement growth (42), 476 

whereas in GLY-AUX2 the oxygenation-derived glycine must satisfy a larger proportion 477 

of cellular nitrogen and one-carbon demands, making GLY-AUX2 a more stringent 478 

selection platform in terms of the absolute Rubisco flux required for detectable growth. 479 

This higher activity threshold is a disadvantage for variants resulting in poor flux 480 

through the module for several reasons e.g., low protein production, solubility, low kcat 481 

in the bottleneck enzyme, or high KM. The difference in selective stringency between 482 

the two strains is worth designing around, and points to the value of their pairing as 483 

demonstrated here: using ∆ rpi to confirm active flux through pCBB2 variants that fail 484 

to complement GLY-AUX2 disambiguates expression or folding failures from genuine 485 

insufficiency of oxygenation activity. 486 

Our ALE campaign nominally selected at ambient air, but glycerol oxidation elevated 487 

dissolved CO 2 in the bioreactor and produced a selection regime in the liquid phase 488 

closer to the elevated-pCO 2 conditions under which Rbc4* subsequently outperformed 489 

wild-type. This is a reminder that the effective selection pressure in continuous 490 

cultivation depends on dissolved pCO 2/pO 2, not just headspace composition. 491

The ability to select directly on Rubisco's oxygenase activity opens lines of 492 

investigation that have been difficult to pursue with carboxylation-coupled assays 493 

alone. Oxygenation is the less-studied of Rubisco's two reactions: it is slow, 494 

thermodynamically wasteful, and historically assayed only in vitro under conditions 495 

that incompletely recapitulate the cellular environment. The GLY-AUX2 strain places 496 

oxygenation under direct growth selection, making fitness a proxy for this reaction in 497 

a living cell. This enables systematic mutational dissection of the sequence 498 

determinants of oxygenase activity across Form II and, in principle, Form I499 

phylogenies, a landscape that in vitro surveys have only partially resolved (6, 7, 17, 500 

22, 26, 51, 59), and one that is particularly relevant to understanding how Rubisco has 501 

evolved under varying atmospheric O 2 and CO 2 regimes (8, 26, 60-62). Because the 502 

assay is bacterial and growth-coupled, it scales to library sizes inaccessible to in vitro 503 

kinetics, making the molecular basis of oxygenation experimentally tractable at a 504 

resolution not previously available. By the same token, the same growth coupling that 505 

we use here to select for oxygenation could be in principle inverted to select against 506 

it, providing a forward-genetic route toward Rubisco variants with suppressed 507 

oxygenase activity — a long-standing goal of enzymology, synthetic biology, plant 508 

sciences, and crop engineering (4, 14, 63, 64). Pursuing these directions will require 509 

pairing GLY-AUX2 with higher-throughput aerated cultivation and with structural and 510 

computational analyses that connect in vivo fitness to active-site geometry, but the 511 

foundation is now in place. 512

## Materials and methods 525 

Modelling of Rubisco oxygenation activity 526 

In order to calculate phenotypic phase planes (rubisco carboxylation flux vs. biomass 527 

production) for two the rubisco-growth-coupled mutants, we adjusted the recently 528 

published iCH360 E. coli model (39) by adding the following relevant reactions (along 529 

with their reactants, when necessary): phosphoribulokinase (PRK), rubisco 530 

carboxylation and rubisco oxygenation (RBC and RBC_OX), the two Entner-Doudoroff 531 

reactions (EDD and EDA), phosphoglycolate phosphatase (PGLYCP), glycolate 532 

oxidase (GLYCTO1), and glycine-oxaloacetate transaminase (GLYOAT). 533 

We then used the COBRApy package (65) to calculate the phenotypic phase planes 534 

for all combinations of the following parameters: (i) carbon source - glycerol, xylose, 535 

or glucose, (i) mutant - ΔRPI ΔEDD ΔEDA (i.e.  Δrpi in short) or  ΔICL ΔGHMT2r (i.e. the 536 

GLY-AUX2), (iii) target reaction - rubisco carboxylation or rubisco oxygenation. When 537 

plotting the phase planes for rubisco carboxylation, the oxygenation reaction was 538 

completely shut off (set both bounds to 0) - and vice versa ( Figure S1 ). 539 

For the rest of the analysis we focused only on the GLY-AUX2 mutant with glycerol as 540 

the carbon source. In order to explore the effect of mixing carboxylation and 541 

oxygenation at different fixed ratios (defined by  φ), we added a linear constraint on a 542 

combination of carboxylation and oxygenation fluxes: v(RBC) - ratio*v(RBC_OX) = 0. 543 

The scalar value "ratio" was set as 10 φ, where  φ acquires different values between -4 544 

and 4. In Fig. 1B , we combined the ratio constraint with an upper bound on the sum 545 

of both rubisco fluxes (set to 1  μmol/min/gCDW). We then maximized growth of the 546 

GLY-AUX2 mutant growing on glycerol (at each  φ). Since carboxylation is counter-547 

productive in this mutant, the function is monotonically increasing with  φ (i.e. more 548 

oxygenation is better). 549 

For generating the phenotypic phase planes for different values of  φ (Extended Data 550 

Fig. 1A ), each phase plane is computed by sweeping the flux in RBC, while again 551 

pinning the flux ratio between v(RBC_OX) and v(RBC) to 10 φ. In this case, the total 552 

Rubisco flux is RBC (1 + 10 φ). After computing each phase plane, its y-axis was 553 

rescaled to show the total Rubisco flux (instead of just the carboxylation alone). For 554 

more details regarding the modelling of Rubisco activity, see the Jupyter notebook 555 

"rubisco_phase_planes.ipynb" in our GitHub repository. 556
